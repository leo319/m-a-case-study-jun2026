# Final memo — UniFirst (Cintas / UniFirst)

*Cintas's $5.5B acquisition of UniFirst — final IC memo, post red-team (verified claims only)*

## Executive summary

Cintas is acquiring UniFirst — the #1 operator buying one of only three scaled national players — for $310.00 per share in cash and stock, an enterprise value of approximately $5.5 billion [1.1]. Each UniFirst share converts into $155.00 cash plus 0.7720 of a Cintas share [2.1]. UniFirst shareholders approved the deal on June 12, 2026 [3], and it now sits under an FTC Second Request issued June 11, 2026 [4.1]. The $310 price is a premium of approximately 102.5% to UniFirst's unaffected price of $153.05 [4.2].

> **Our view —** the strategic logic is real and the cost lever is genuine, but Cintas is paying a full-to-aggressive price that leaves little margin for error: $310 is above the high end of every standalone valuation method, and the c. 102.5% premium is c. 2.85x the precedent median — a genuine, steep premium to a clean unaffected price.

On whether it closes: the shareholder vote is already done and the financing is committed, so the deal now turns almost entirely on antitrust. On balance we think it more likely closes than not — a four-year pursuit, a $350M reverse fee with an obligation to litigate any challenge, and a favorable clearance base rate weigh toward approval — but the most likely path is that Cintas first agrees to sell off some overlapping local operations to satisfy the FTC, and there is a real chance the deal is blocked or significantly delayed. The FTC has so far only issued a Second Request [4.1]; it has not required any remedy.

> **Our view —** the risks that most threaten the thesis are, in order: antitrust — the Second Request in a roughly half-market 3-to-2 with an impaired divestiture buyer; overpayment if synergies disappoint; and erosion of UniFirst's fundamentals during a long pendency.

## Company overview

UniFirst, the target, is one of the leading North American providers of workplace uniforms and protective clothing, serving over 300,000 customer locations across the U.S., Canada and Europe [5.1], with approximately 16,000 employees [5.2]. It is unusually vertically integrated, self-manufacturing about 62% of the garments it places in service [5.3]. FY2025 revenue was essentially flat at c. $2,432.4M [5.4] and operating income was c. $184.5M [5.5] — a c. 7.6% operating margin, far below Cintas's c. 22.8% [6.1]. Revenue is about 92% U.S. (c. $2,245.9M of c. $2,432.4M) [5.6].

Cintas, the acquirer, is North America's leading provider of uniform rental and facility services [7.1], with FY2025 revenue of c. $10,340.2M (up 7.7%) [6.2] and operating income of c. $2.36 billion — a c. 22.8% margin [6.1] — across c. 48,300 employees [7.2]. It operates at far greater scale than UniFirst: about 12,100 local delivery routes from 478 facilities, with c. 22,900 vehicles [7.3], against UniFirst's roughly 4,813 vehicles [5.10].

## Industry & market

Uniform rental and facility services is a route-based, recurring-revenue business. Both companies describe their markets as local in nature and highly fragmented [7.4]. In the U.S. and Canada the market is effectively a three-national-player structure, with all three public participants naming the same short list of national rivals — Cintas, UniFirst and Vestis — over a long regional tail [5.11], and customer concentration is low (no Cintas customer is more than 1% of revenue) [7.5]. The landscape was reshaped by the 2023 spin-off of Vestis from Aramark [8]; Vestis calls itself the #2 in the U.S. and Canada [9.1] but has since deteriorated — FY2025 revenue of c. $2.7B at a 2.4% operating margin and a net loss of $40.2M [9.2], with rental declines where lost business exceeded new business [9.3].

> **Our view —** the persistence is about scarcity, not opportunism. UniFirst is the last scaled, independent, family-controlled operator, Cintas chased it for about four years escalating $255 to $275 to $310, and the move reads as offensive consolidation of a scarce asset with a defensive element — a weakened Vestis and an activist opening.

> **Our view —** but the family did not simply decide to sell. Its board defeated Engine Capital's board slate on December 15, 2025 with its voting control intact, then agreed in February 2026 to $310 — about 13% above the $275 it had repeatedly called full and fair. The better-supported read is that it agreed under activist pressure at a higher price, not because it lost control — so the sale was at least partly pressured, and part of the premium looks like the price to resolve the activist situation rather than pure scarcity value.

## Deal structure

Each UniFirst share converts into $155.00 cash plus 0.7720 Cintas shares [2.1], implying about 14.26M Cintas shares issued [2.2]. After closing, existing Cintas shareholders will own approximately 96.6% of the combined company and former UniFirst holders approximately 3.4% [15.1] — so this is predominantly a cash deal with a small stock component: UniFirst holders mostly cash out and take only a thin minority equity stake. The cash leg is funded from cash on hand and committed credit, with no financing condition [4.4], backstopped by a committed bridge from Morgan Stanley, KeyBank and Wells Fargo [1.2]; net leverage at close is guided to c. 1.5x [1.3].

Closing conditions are HSR clearance plus a foreign-regulator clearance and permit consents [4.5][4.6], UniFirst's (now obtained) shareholder approval [4.7], and NASDAQ listing of the new shares [4.8]; both sides carry MAE bring-downs [4.9][4.10] and there is a no-shop covenant [4.11]. Termination fees are $213.3M payable by UniFirst and a $350M reverse fee payable by Cintas [16], the deal is specifically enforceable [4.12] — meaning a court can compel the parties to complete the merger, rather than letting a party walk away by paying the break fee — and the outside date is extendable [4.13]. The outside date is the deadline by which the deal must close or either side may walk away; here it can be pushed out, to allow more time for antitrust clearance.

> **Our view —** the agreement shifts a defined slice of antitrust risk to Cintas — the $350M reverse fee (about 1.6x the target fee) plus an obligation to litigate challenges — but stops short of an unlimited 'hell or high water' commitment, so UniFirst retains residual blocked-deal risk; and while vote risk is low, it sits on a related-party footing, since the same controllers the activist criticized delivered the approval.

## Strategic & financial rationale

**How the business makes money.** Revenue is recurring weekly route servicing: as part of the rental business the operator picks up a customer's soiled uniforms and other items, usually weekly, and delivers back cleaned and processed replacement items [5.12]. About 84% of UniFirst's revenue is route-servicing fees [5.13], and roughly 95% of Cintas's revenue is route servicing across uniforms, facility services, first aid and fire [7.6]. The cost of providing that service — UniFirst's cost of revenues was c. $1,542.4M, or 63.4% of revenue, its single largest cost line [5.14] — is the in-plant processing (laundering) labor, energy for plants and the delivery fleet, route pick-up and delivery, and amortization of the merchandise placed in service [5.12][5.15]; UniFirst spreads the cost of the uniforms and other merchandise it puts into service across their expected useful life of roughly six months to three years, rather than expensing it all at once [5.16]. SG&A was c. $565.1M, or 23.2% of revenue [5.17], and consists of the sales force, corporate and administrative labor, and public-company overhead — for the comparable Cintas line, sales labor and commissions, management and administrative labor, insurance, and legal and professional costs [7.7].

> **Our view —** profitability in this business is governed by route density: those processing, energy and delivery costs are largely fixed per route and per plant, so serving more stops on each route and pushing more volume through each plant lowers the cost per stop. Density, not the size of any single account, is the lever on margin.

**The customer base.** Both companies serve businesses of all sizes [7.5][5.18], and a large share of the route-servicing revenue is non-uniform services — facility, first aid and fire [7.6]. Those non-uniform lines are: facility services (floor mats, mops, restroom supplies and cleaning); first aid and safety (stocking and maintaining first-aid cabinets, safety supplies and AEDs); and fire protection (inspecting and servicing fire extinguishers, alarms and sprinkler systems). Cintas's December 2025 investor presentation frames how small a share of the market the players reach: management states the U.S. and Canada hold 180M+ workers and 16M businesses, against combined Cintas-plus-UniFirst penetration today of fewer than 8M wearers and fewer than 2M customers — so the combined company reaches only a small fraction of the potential market, leaving a lot of room to grow.

> **Our view —** the implication is the part that matters. Even the three national players together reach only a small share of this huge market; the rest is a long tail of small regional providers [5.19][7.4]. Two things follow. First, there is a large untapped growth runway — the bases of over a million customers each are tiny against 16M businesses [7.5]. Second, the scaled players' route density, breadth of services and capital give them a structural cost-and-service edge over the fragmented small competitors, which is how they keep taking share. That is consistent with a low average of uniform wearers per customer: a low count per customer just means each customer is small — a handful of uniformed staff — which fits a fragmented base in which no customer is a large share [7.5]. The cost advantage comes from serving many small customers (stops) per route — route density — not from more wearers per customer (see the synergy lever below).

**The synergy lever.** Cintas asserts c. $375M of run-rate operating cost synergies within four years [1.4], embedded in the stated 8.0x EV/EBITDA multiple [1.5].

> **Our view —** the lever is structurally plausible: c. $375M must come out of UniFirst's cost of revenues (63.4%) and SG&A (23.2%), and because the two carriers run overlapping local route networks, combining them raises stops per route and plant utilization, gives more procurement scale on merchandise and energy, and removes duplicate corporate and public-company overhead. The overlap that matters here is geographic, not customer-by-customer: Cintas and UniFirst run delivery routes through many of the same cities while serving largely different businesses, so combining them lets a single route and plant cover more stops in a territory. The gain comes from denser routes, not from selling more to shared customers — so the two firms serving different accounts in the same places is the opportunity, not an obstacle.

> **Our view —** one tension to flag: the overlapping local routes that create the density savings are the same routes where the combined market share is highest, which is exactly what the FTC is most likely to make Cintas divest (see tail risks). So an antitrust remedy would not just trim the deal in general — it would cut into the specific local density the synergies depend on.

**The supply chains do not match.** UniFirst self-manufactures about 62% of garments it places in service, primarily at owned plants in Mexico and Nicaragua [5.20][5.21], while Cintas sources finished product from many outside suppliers and operates only five manufacturing plants [7.8]. UniFirst flags that trade policy and tariffs on imported goods could materially affect its business [5.22].

> **Our view —** Cintas is buying routes, customers and density, not manufacturing capacity it chose for years not to own. The captive plants are an incidental byproduct of the target — a likely integration complication and a rationalization candidate, plus inherited tariff exposure and a second sourcing-and-systems stack — not a driver of the deal.

> **Our view —** the price is full-to-aggressive: $310 is above the high end of every method above, from c. 16% over J.P. Morgan's DCF top to c. 70% over its trading-multiples top, and the c. 102.5% premium is c. 2.85x the 36% precedent median. The stated 8.0x EV/EBITDA understates what Cintas is paying, because it is struck including the c. $375M synergies — back them out and the true ex-synergy entry multiple is materially higher than 8.0x.

> **Our view —** that 102.5% premium is the genuine premium, and the $153.05 baseline is credible, not suspect. $153.05 is the price the day before UniFirst disclosed the activist's director nominations [4.2], and Cintas had already terminated its prior bid in March 2025 [4.19] — so the prior approach was dead and the activist fight was not yet public. The stock had in fact fallen below its early-2025 pre-bid level: Cintas's January 2025 $275 bid was a 46% premium to UniFirst's 90-day average [13.2], implying a pre-bid level of roughly $188, well above the $153.05 unaffected price — so little takeover speculation was embedded in the baseline. The c. 102.5% premium is real and steep.

**Financing and accretion.** The deal is funded with about $2.8B of new debt at an assumed 5% rate [15.2][15.3]; Cintas guides to EPS accretion only by the end of the second full year after closing [1.3], at c. 1.5x leverage [1.3].

> **Our view —** the synergy target is large — c. 2.03x UniFirst's operating income and c. 15.4% of its revenue — so the value case depends on capturing it; but it is achievable conditional on execution, because Cintas's G&K precedent (a $130-140M synergy target whose integration wound down cleanly) is base-rate evidence it can run laundry-consolidation cost synergies. The prize is the margin gap (UniFirst c. 7.6% vs Cintas c. 22.8%), which the activist case — an interested party whose proposed remedy was a sale — suggests is at least partly an execution problem a better operator can address.

> **Our view —** two caveats on the synergy bet. The G&K deal shows Cintas can integrate a laundry acquisition cleanly, but not that it can capture a synergy of this relative size: $375M is about 2.0x UniFirst's entire operating income and roughly a quarter of its cost of revenues, a much larger extraction than G&K's. And Cintas guides EPS accretion by the end of year two, while the synergies are guided to full run-rate only in year four — so the early accretion leans on partial synergies and the financing structure, not the full number.

## Tail risks

Ranked by what most threatens the thesis.

**1. Antitrust — the dominant risk to closing.** The FTC issued a Second Request on June 11, 2026 [4.1]. Trade-press analysis estimates the combined entity at 45-50% of the North American market [17] and characterizes the deal as a 3-to-2 of the Big Three [17]. Two comparable precedents cleared — though this is a more concentrated, 3-to-2 combination [17], so they are favorable but imperfect comparators: Cintas/G&K (2016, second request, cleared with no divestitures) [11] and Aramark/AmeriPride (HSR early termination, 2017) [12]. But the most plausible national divestiture buyer, Vestis, was downgraded to 'B' by S&P in December 2025 amid customer losses [18].

> **Our view —** the base rate is favorable, but an impaired divestiture buyer is the exact condition that can push the FTC from accepting divestitures toward challenging the deal. On the base rate and the trade-press read [18][17], our judgment is that the likely remedy is local route and branch divestitures, with a real, non-negligible block-or-abandon tail — though the FTC has so far only issued a Second Request [4.1] and has not required any remedy.

**2. Overpayment.** As the valuation table above shows, the $310 offer sits above the high end of all six standalone methods from both advisors — from c. 16% over J.P. Morgan's DCF top to c. 70% over its trading-multiples top [4.2][4.15]. The c. $375M synergy target Cintas asserts [1.4] is roughly 2x UniFirst's c. $184.5M FY2025 operating income [5.5], and Cintas guides to EPS accretion only by the end of the second full year after closing [1.7].

> **Our view —** the price exceeds every standalone valuation method, so the value case rests on synergies; if synergy capture underdelivers or antitrust divestitures shrink the prize, Cintas has overpaid, and accretion — already pushed to year two — depends on the synergies landing.

**3. Erosion of UniFirst's fundamentals before closing — the highest-consequence tail.** UniFirst's revenue was essentially flat in FY2025 at c. $2,432.4M [5.4], with operating income of c. $184.5M [5.5] — a c. 7.6% operating margin, about a third of Cintas's c. 22.8% [6.1]. The activist asserts UniFirst can no longer close the widening competitive gap with Cintas and that continued share losses will drive declining revenue [19.1]. UniFirst serves customers under three-to-five-year route contracts, so attrition shows up as non-renewals [5.23].

> **Our view —** the FTC's Second Request can stretch the review across several more quarters, and a route-based business is exposed during that wait: customers on multi-year contracts may decline to renew, and drivers and salespeople may leave, while the deal's outcome is uncertain. If UniFirst's business weakens before the deal closes, both the synergy base and the standalone value Cintas agreed to pay for shrink.

**4. Integration and execution.** UniFirst entered the deal with an unremediated IT material weakness in its general controls [5.24] and a multiyear ERP program running to 2027 [5.25]. The proxy names retention of key employees as a risk to realizing the deal's benefits [2.3], and the two run different supply-chain models [5.20][7.8].

> **Our view —** this runs above a normal bolt-on: a long pendency widens the window over which route drivers, the sales force and customers can be lost, and that attrition erodes the synergy base itself.

**5. Litigation — low.** Both companies report only ordinary-course claims, and no lawsuits challenging the merger had been filed as of the proxy date [4.20]. Two unsized contingent items sit outside the ordinary course: UniFirst's unresolved Mexican tax assessment, still on appeal [5.26], and a Cintas reference to litigation outside the ordinary course whose size it does not disclose [7.9].

**6. Financing — low, but not zero.** The cash portion is backed by a committed bridge with no financing condition [1.2][4.4]. The residual risk is that Cintas had not secured the c. $2.8B of permanent debt as of the S-4, and because its obligation to close is not conditioned on financing yet is specifically enforceable [4.12], adverse credit markets could force it onto the costlier bridge or into specific performance rather than a walk-away.

**Governance and the activist — examined, but not a risk to this deal.** Engine Capital (c. 3.2% of UniFirst) ran a proxy contest in late 2025 attacking the company's weak shareholder returns [19.2] and a margin decline from 12.8% in 2019 to a projected 6.4% in FY2026 [19.3], and pushed the board to sell — winning the support of all three leading proxy advisors [20]. Because the activist wanted a sale and the Croatti family's voting lock-up secured shareholder approval, this standard tail-risk area cuts in favor of the deal, not against it: it helps explain why the board agreed (see 'Industry & market' above) and it lowers vote risk rather than adding to it. No short-seller report on either company was found.

## Assessment & what would change our view

**Scope and limits of this assessment.** This covers the deal's strategic and financial rationale, its tail risks, and how likely it is to close. It is not a standalone investment or 'good deal' judgment, and it does not include a detailed merger-agreement and legal review, a tax analysis, or a test of management's own projections — any of which could move the conclusion. Some figures are not fully sourced and are flagged as such in the text: the split of the c. $375M synergy across route, plant and merchandise costs is not disclosed (it is our reasoned estimate), the 45-50% combined-share figure is a trade-press estimate, and the market-size and wearers-per-customer figures come from Cintas's investor presentation (management's numbers, not independently verified).

**Will it close?** Two of the three gates are already cleared: 

1) UniFirst shareholders approved the deal on June 12, 2026 [3] — the Croatti family's lock-up made that outcome certain [4.21] 

2) The financing is committed with no financing condition [4.4]. 

The deal now turns almost entirely on antitrust. Weighing toward approval are Cintas's four-year pursuit and the determination it implies [4.22], a $350M reverse fee payable if the deal is blocked plus an obligation to litigate any challenge [4.13], and a favorable clearance base rate, since G&K and AmeriPride both cleared [11][12]. 

Weighing against are the FTC Second Request [4.1], a roughly half-market 3-to-2 structure [17], and an impaired divestiture buyer in Vestis [18].

> **Our view —** more likely to close than not, but probably not cleanly. The most likely path — on the base rate and the trade-press read [18][17] — is that the FTC requires Cintas to divest operations in the local markets where the combined share would be too high, as the price of approval; so far the FTC has only issued a Second Request [4.1] and required no remedy. The complication is that the most obvious buyer for those divested routes, Vestis, is financially weak, so a clean buyer may be hard to find — which is what could turn a divest-and-clear outcome into an FTC challenge. A real chance of a block or a long delay remains.

**What to watch — and what would change this view.** (i) The FTC Second Request outcome — the scope of any required divestitures versus an outright challenge; a large divestiture demand or a suit to block lowers the odds of closing. (ii) Whether a credible buyer emerges for any divested routes — the harder that is, the higher the block risk. (iii) UniFirst's revenue and customer/employee retention during the long review — a sharp deterioration could give Cintas a reason to walk or renegotiate, and weakens both the standalone value and the synergy base.
