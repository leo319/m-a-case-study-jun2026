# Preliminary memo — UniFirst (Cintas / UniFirst)

_Cintas's $5.5B acquisition of UniFirst — preliminary IC memo (verified claims only)_

## Executive summary

Cintas is acquiring UniFirst — the #1 operator buying one of only three scaled national players — for $310.00 per share in cash and stock, an enterprise value of approximately $5.5 billion [1.1]. Each UniFirst share converts into $155.00 cash plus 0.7720 of a Cintas share [2.1]. UniFirst shareholders approved the deal on June 12, 2026 [3], and it now sits under an FTC Second Request issued June 11, 2026 [4.1]. The $310 price is a premium of approximately 102.5% to UniFirst's unaffected price of $153.05 [4.2].

> **Our view —** the strategic logic is real and the cost lever is genuine, but Cintas is paying a full-to-aggressive price that leaves little margin for error: $310 is above the high end of every standalone valuation method, and the c. 102.5% premium is c. 2.85x the precedent median.

We keep two questions apart. On whether it closes: more likely than not — a four-year pursuit, a family vote lock-up, a $350M reverse fee with an obligation to litigate antitrust challenges, and a favorable clearance base rate outweigh the antitrust pull — but it most likely closes only after Cintas agrees to sell off some overlapping local operations to satisfy the FTC, and there is a real chance it is blocked or significantly delayed. On whether it is a good deal: only if the c. $375M of synergies land near target, because the price already capitalizes them.

> **Our view —** the risks that most threaten the thesis are, in order: antitrust — the Second Request in a roughly half-market 3-to-2 with an impaired divestiture buyer; overpayment if synergies disappoint; and erosion of UniFirst's fundamentals during a long pendency.

## Company overview

UniFirst, the target, is one of the leading North American providers of workplace uniforms and protective clothing, serving over 300,000 customer locations across the U.S., Canada and Europe [5.1], with approximately 16,000 employees [5.2]. It is unusually vertically integrated, self-manufacturing about 62% of the garments it places in service [5.3]. FY2025 revenue was essentially flat at c. $2,432.4M [5.4] and operating income was c. $184.5M [5.5] — a c. 7.6% operating margin, far below Cintas's c. 22.8% [6.1]. Revenue is about 92% U.S. (c. $2,245.9M of c. $2,432.4M) [5.6].

Cintas, the acquirer, is North America's leading provider of uniform rental and facility services [7.1], with FY2025 revenue of c. $10,340.2M (up 7.7%) [6.2] and operating income of c. $2.36 billion — a c. 22.8% margin [6.1] — across c. 48,300 employees [7.2]. It operates at far greater scale than UniFirst: about 12,100 local delivery routes from 478 facilities, with c. 22,900 vehicles [7.3], against UniFirst's roughly 4,813 vehicles [5.10].

## Industry & market

Uniform rental and facility services is a route-based, recurring-revenue business. Both companies describe their markets as local in nature and highly fragmented [7.4]. In the U.S. and Canada the market is effectively a three-national-player structure, with all three public participants naming the same short list of national rivals — Cintas, UniFirst and Vestis — over a long regional tail [5.11], and customer concentration is low (no Cintas customer is more than 1% of revenue) [7.5]. The landscape was reshaped by the 2023 spin-off of Vestis from Aramark [8]; Vestis calls itself the #2 in the U.S. and Canada [9.1] but has since deteriorated — FY2025 revenue of c. $2.7B at a 2.4% operating margin and a net loss of $40.2M [9.2], with rental declines where lost business exceeded new business [9.3].

> **Our view —** the persistence is about scarcity, not opportunism. UniFirst is the last scaled, independent, family-controlled operator, Cintas chased it for about four years escalating $255 to $275 to $310, and the move reads as offensive consolidation of a scarce asset with a defensive element — a weakened Vestis and an activist opening.

## Deal structure

Each UniFirst share converts into $155.00 cash plus 0.7720 Cintas shares [2.1], implying about 14.26M Cintas shares issued [2.2]. After closing, existing Cintas shareholders will own approximately 96.6% of the combined company and former UniFirst holders approximately 3.4% [15.1] — so this is predominantly a cash deal with a small stock component: UniFirst holders mostly cash out and take only a thin minority equity stake. The cash leg is funded from cash on hand and committed credit, with no financing condition [4.4], backstopped by a committed bridge from Morgan Stanley, KeyBank and Wells Fargo [1.2]; net leverage at close is guided to c. 1.5x [1.3].

Closing conditions are HSR clearance plus a foreign-regulator clearance and permit consents [4.5][4.6], UniFirst's (now obtained) shareholder approval [4.7], and NASDAQ listing of the new shares [4.8]; both sides carry MAE bring-downs [4.9][4.10] and there is a no-shop covenant [4.11]. Termination fees are $213.3M payable by UniFirst and a $350M reverse fee payable by Cintas [16], the deal is specifically enforceable [4.12], and the outside date is extendable [4.13].

> **Our view —** the agreement shifts a defined slice of antitrust risk to Cintas — the $350M reverse fee (about 1.6x the target fee) plus an obligation to litigate challenges — but stops short of an unlimited 'hell or high water' commitment, so UniFirst retains residual blocked-deal risk; and while vote risk is low, it sits on a related-party footing, since the same controllers the activist criticized delivered the approval.

## Strategic & financial rationale

**How the business makes money.** Revenue is recurring weekly route servicing: as part of the rental business the operator picks up a customer's soiled uniforms and other items, usually weekly, and delivers back cleaned and processed replacement items [5.12]. About 84% of UniFirst's revenue is route-servicing fees [5.13], and roughly 95% of Cintas's revenue is route servicing across uniforms, facility services, first aid and fire [7.6]. The cost of providing that service — UniFirst's cost of revenues was c. $1,542.4M, or 63.4% of revenue, its single largest cost line [5.14] — is the in-plant processing (laundering) labor, energy for plants and the delivery fleet, route pick-up and delivery, and amortization of the merchandise placed in service [5.12][5.15]; UniFirst spreads the cost of the uniforms and other merchandise it puts into service across their expected useful life of roughly six months to three years, rather than expensing it all at once [5.16]. SG&A was c. $565.1M, or 23.2% of revenue [5.17], and consists of the sales force, corporate and administrative labor, and public-company overhead — for the comparable Cintas line, sales labor and commissions, management and administrative labor, insurance, and legal and professional costs [7.7].

> **Our view —** profitability in this business is governed by route density: those processing, energy and delivery costs are largely fixed per route and per plant, so serving more stops on each route and pushing more volume through each plant lowers the cost per stop. Density, not the size of any single account, is the lever on margin.

**The customer base.** Both companies serve businesses of all sizes [7.5][5.18], and a large share of the route-servicing revenue is non-uniform services — facility, first aid and fire [7.6]. Cintas's December 2025 investor presentation indicates a long runway on this base: management states the U.S. and Canada hold 180M+ workers and 16M businesses, against combined Cintas-plus-UniFirst penetration today of fewer than 8M wearers and fewer than 2M customers, which implies an average of roughly four uniform wearers per customer.

> **Our view —** that low wearers-per-customer figure is consistent with the verified facts and supports the density thesis rather than undermining it: a base of over a million mostly small accounts, many buying non-uniform route services with few or no uniformed staff, is exactly the broad base of many small stops in which density is the cost lever.

**The synergy lever.** Cintas asserts c. $375M of run-rate operating cost synergies within four years [1.4], embedded in the stated 8.0x EV/EBITDA multiple [1.5].

> **Our view —** the lever is structurally plausible: c. $375M must come out of UniFirst's cost of revenues (63.4%) and SG&A (23.2%), and because the two carriers run overlapping local route networks, combining them raises stops per route and plant utilization, gives more procurement scale on merchandise and energy, and removes duplicate corporate and public-company overhead. The overlap that matters here is geographic, not customer-by-customer: Cintas and UniFirst run delivery routes through many of the same cities while serving largely different businesses, so combining them lets a single route and plant cover more stops in a territory. The gain comes from denser routes, not from selling more to shared customers — so the two firms serving different accounts in the same places is the opportunity, not an obstacle.

**The supply chains do not match.** UniFirst self-manufactures about 62% of garments it places in service, primarily at owned plants in Mexico and Nicaragua [5.19][5.20], while Cintas sources finished product from many outside suppliers and operates only five manufacturing plants [7.8]. UniFirst flags that trade policy and tariffs on imported goods could materially affect its business [5.21].

> **Our view —** Cintas is buying routes, customers and density, not manufacturing capacity it chose for years not to own. The captive plants are an incidental byproduct of the target — a likely integration complication and a rationalization candidate, plus inherited tariff exposure and a second sourcing-and-systems stack — not a driver of the deal.

> **Our view —** the price is full-to-aggressive: $310 is above the high end of every method above, from c. 16% over J.P. Morgan's DCF top to c. 70% over its trading-multiples top, and the c. 102.5% premium is c. 2.85x the 36% precedent median. The stated 8.0x EV/EBITDA understates what Cintas is paying, because it is struck including the c. $375M synergies — back them out and the true ex-synergy entry multiple is materially higher than 8.0x.

**Financing and accretion.** The deal is funded with about $2.8B of new debt at an assumed 5% rate [15.2][15.3]; Cintas guides to EPS accretion only by the end of the second full year after closing [1.3], at c. 1.5x leverage [1.3].

> **Our view —** the synergy target is large — c. 2.03x UniFirst's operating income and c. 15.4% of its revenue — so the value case depends on capturing it; but it is achievable conditional on execution, because Cintas's G&K precedent (a $130-140M synergy target whose integration wound down cleanly) is base-rate evidence it can run laundry-consolidation cost synergies. The prize is the margin gap (UniFirst c. 7.6% vs Cintas c. 22.8%), which the activist case suggests is at least partly an execution problem a better operator can address.

## Tail risks

Ranked by what most threatens the thesis.

**1. Antitrust — the dominant risk to closing.** The FTC issued a Second Request on June 11, 2026 [4.1]. Trade-press analysis estimates the combined entity at 45-50% of the North American market [17] and characterizes the deal as a 3-to-2 of the Big Three [17]. Two directly comparable precedents cleared: Cintas/G&K (2016, second request, cleared with no divestitures) [11] and Aramark/AmeriPride (HSR early termination, 2017) [12]. But the most plausible national divestiture buyer, Vestis, was downgraded to 'B' by S&P in December 2025 amid customer losses [18].

> **Our view —** the base rate is favorable, but an impaired divestiture buyer is the exact condition that can push the FTC from accepting divestitures toward challenging the deal; the realistic path is local route and branch divestitures, with a real, non-negligible block-or-abandon tail.

**2. Overpayment.** As the valuation table above shows, the $310 offer sits above the high end of all six standalone methods from both advisors — from c. 16% over J.P. Morgan's DCF top to c. 70% over its trading-multiples top [4.2][4.15]. The c. $375M synergy target Cintas asserts [1.4] is roughly 2x UniFirst's c. $184.5M FY2025 operating income [5.5], and Cintas guides to EPS accretion only by the end of the second full year after closing [1.7].

> **Our view —** the price exceeds every standalone valuation method, so the value case rests on synergies; if synergy capture underdelivers or antitrust divestitures shrink the prize, Cintas has overpaid, and accretion — already pushed to year two — depends on the synergies landing.

**3. Erosion of UniFirst's fundamentals before closing — the highest-consequence tail.** UniFirst's revenue was essentially flat in FY2025 at c. $2,432.4M [5.4], with operating income of c. $184.5M [5.5] — a c. 7.6% operating margin, about a third of Cintas's c. 22.8% [6.1]. The activist asserts UniFirst can no longer close the widening competitive gap with Cintas and that continued share losses will drive declining revenue [19.1]. UniFirst serves customers under three-to-five-year route contracts, so attrition shows up as non-renewals [5.22].

> **Our view —** a route-based business under a multi-quarter Second Request is exactly where customers and people bleed; if fundamentals erode during the long pendency, both the synergy base and the standalone value Cintas agreed to pay shrink together.

**4. Integration and execution.** UniFirst entered the deal with an unremediated IT material weakness in its general controls [5.23] and a multiyear ERP program running to 2027 [5.24]. The proxy names retention of key employees as a risk to realizing the deal's benefits [2.3], and the two run different supply-chain models [5.19][7.8].

> **Our view —** this runs above a normal bolt-on: a long pendency widens the window over which route drivers, the sales force and customers can be lost, and that attrition erodes the synergy base itself.

**5. Governance and activist.** Engine Capital (c. 3.2% of UniFirst) ran a proxy contest asserting deeply disappointing returns [19.2] and a margin decline from 12.8% in 2019 to a projected 6.4% in FY2026 [19.3], winning ISS support [20] before the board's slate prevailed at the December 15, 2025 meeting [21]; all three leading proxy advisors had backed the activist [22]. This is an activist proxy contest, not a short-seller report.

**6. Lower-ranked.** Litigation is clean — only ordinary-course claims, and no lawsuits challenging the merger as of the proxy date [4.19]. Financing risk is low — a committed bridge and no financing condition [1.2][4.4].

## Assessment & what would change our view

We keep the two questions strictly apart.

**Will it close?** The forces pushing it through are a four-year strategic pursuit that culminated in Cintas paying $35 above the price it had insisted was full and fair [4.20], the Croatti family's voting-and-support lock-up that makes approval effectively certain [4.21], a $350M reverse fee payable if the deal is blocked on antitrust grounds plus an obligation to litigate any challenge [4.13], committed financing with no financing condition [4.4], and a favorable clearance base rate, since G&K and AmeriPride both cleared [11][12]. Pulling against are the FTC Second Request [4.1], a roughly half-market 3-to-2 structure [17], and an impaired divestiture buyer in Vestis [18].

> **Our view —** on balance, we think it more likely closes than not — but probably not cleanly. The most likely path is that the FTC requires Cintas to sell off operations in the local markets where the combined share would be too high, as the price of approval. The catch is that the obvious buyer for those cast-off routes, Vestis, is financially weak, so a clean buyer may be hard to find — and that is what could turn 'sell some routes and clear' into 'the FTC sues to block the deal.' A real chance of a block or a long delay remains.

**Is it a good deal?** A separate question. The deal is conservatively financed, and the $310 offer is above the high end of every standalone valuation method [4.2][4.15], so value for Cintas requires capturing most of the c. $375M of asserted synergies [1.4].

> **Our view —** only if the synergies land near target. Strategically logical, but priced above standalone value, so value creation hinges on synergy capture — which the G&K precedent says Cintas can do, while the full price leaves little room if it cannot. A deal can be both likely to close and likely to disappoint. Here we are more confident that the deal will close than that it will turn out to be a good investment for Cintas.

**Net call.** A strategically coherent consolidation that should ultimately clear, probably with divestitures, and create value if Cintas executes the route-density playbook it has run before — at a price that already assumes most of those synergies will be captured.

**Scope of this verdict.** This assessment rests only on the strategic and financial rationale and the tail risks analyzed here. Other important inputs were not examined in depth and could move the conclusion — notably a detailed merger-agreement and deal-terms analysis (MAE carve-outs, the precise divestiture / regulatory-efforts mechanics, the calendar outside date), tax, and management's own financial projections.

**Monitorables, and what would flip them.** (i) The FTC Second Request outcome — the scope of any divestitures versus a challenge; a large divestiture or a block lowers close-odds and shrinks the prize. (ii) Synergy-realization markers post-close — tracking toward $375M lifts the value case, slippage confirms overpayment. (iii) UniFirst's revenue and retention during pendency — deterioration hits both value and synergies. (iv) Integration signals — IT/ERP remediation and route-driver and sales-force retention.

**Limitations.** The precise split of the c. $375M synergy across route, plant and merchandise dollars, and the per-stop economics, are not disclosed in the filings and remain a reasoned judgment rather than a cited figure. Market-share and penetration figures (the 45-50% combined share) are trade-press and analyst estimates. The wearers-per-customer and total-addressable-market figures (180M+ workers, 16M businesses, fewer than 8M combined wearers, fewer than 2M combined customers) come from Cintas's December 2025 investor presentation, which is an image-PDF the verifier cannot machine-check, so those deck figures are attributed to management in prose and are deliberately not carried as cited facts; the inference built on them rests on the verified customer-base facts instead. A few merger-agreement fine points (MAE carve-outs, the calendar outside date) did not extract cleanly from the source and are not asserted here.
