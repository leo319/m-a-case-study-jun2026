# Research Brief — Cintas Corporation's Acquisition of UniFirst Corporation

_Stage-3 grounded research brief — all load-bearing facts cite verified, source-checked claims. Analytical judgments are deferred to the IC memo._

## Executive summary

On March 10, 2026, Cintas Corporation (NASDAQ: CTAS) signed a definitive agreement to acquire UniFirst Corporation (NYSE: UNF) for **$310.00 per share in cash and stock**, an enterprise value of approximately **$5.5 billion** [1.1]. Each UniFirst share converts into **$155.00 in cash plus 0.7720 of a Cintas share** [2.1], leaving former UniFirst holders with roughly **3.4%** of the combined company [2.2][3.1]. UniFirst shareholders approved the merger on June 12, 2026 [4]; the parties continue to target a close in the second half of calendar 2026 [5].

This brings together the **#1 and a leading-but-smaller player** in North American uniform rental and facility services. Cintas is the clear scale leader — c. $10.34 billion of FY2025 revenue at a c. 22.8% operating margin [6.1][6.2] — while UniFirst earned c. $184.5 million of operating income on c. $2.43 billion of revenue, a c. 7.6% margin, roughly a third of Cintas's [7.1][7.2].

Three threads dominate the file and frame the work for the IC memo:
- **Price and synergies.** The $310 price is a c. 102.5% premium to the proxy's stated unaffected price of $153.05 and sits **above the high end of every standalone valuation methodology** produced by both financial advisors. Cintas asserts c. $375 million of run-rate cost synergies [1.2] — roughly **double UniFirst's entire FY2025 operating income** — and its headline 8.0x EV/EBITDA multiple is struck **including** those synergies [1.3].
- **Antitrust.** The combination has drawn an **FTC Second Request**, issued at the expiry of the refiled HSR waiting period on June 11, 2026 [8.1], after a "pull-and-refile" [8.1]. It is characterized in trade analysis as a 3-to-2 combination of the historical "Big Three" with combined share estimated at 45–50% [9].
- **Governance and the activist.** UniFirst is family-controlled through a dual-class structure giving the Croatti family over two-thirds of the vote [8.2]. The board rejected a $275 all-cash Cintas bid in January 2025 [8.3], then faced an Engine Capital proxy campaign [10.1] before agreeing to the higher cash-and-stock deal in February 2026 [8.4].

## The companies

**UniFirst Corporation (the target).** UniFirst is one of the leading providers of workplace uniforms and protective clothing in North America, serving over 300,000 customer locations across the U.S., Canada and Europe [7.3], with approximately 16,000 "team partners" [7.4]. It is **vertically integrated**, manufacturing approximately 62% of the garments it places in service [7.5]. Revenue is overwhelmingly route-based and recurring, under written service contracts of three-to-five-year duration [7.5]. FY2025 revenue was essentially flat at c. $2,432.4 million [7.2] and operating income was c. $184.5 million, up 0.5% [7.1] — a c. 7.6% operating margin. The company generated c. $296.9 million of operating cash flow [7.6] against c. $154.3 million of capex [7.6] — capital intensity of roughly 52% of operating cash flow. Its stated growth strategy leans on continued acquisitions [7.7], and it is mid-stream on CRM/ERP "Key Initiatives" [7.8]; management says these investments are beginning to show returns [13].

UniFirst's segment revenue mix (FY2025):

|---|---|---|---|
| Uniform & Facility Service Solutions | c. $2,218.6M | c. 91.2% | [7.9][7.10] |
| First Aid & Safety Solutions | c. $114.6M | c. 4.7% | [7.9][7.11] |
| Other | c. $99.2M | c. 4.1% | [7.9] |

By geography, c. $2,245.9M is U.S. with c. $186.5M from Europe and Canada [7.12]; c. 7.0% of revenue is non-USD [7.13]. Normalized for FY2024's extra week, Uniform & Facility organic growth was c. +1.7% and First Aid grew c. +10.0% [7.2].

**Cintas Corporation (the acquirer).** Cintas describes itself as North America's leading provider of corporate identity uniforms through rental and sales programs [14.1], reporting through Uniform Rental & Facility Services and First Aid & Safety Services segments [14.2]. FY2025 (ended May 31, 2025) revenue was c. $10,340.2 million, up 7.7% [6.1], with operating income of c. $2.36 billion — a c. 22.8% margin [6.2]. It employs approximately 48,300 [14.3] and characterizes its diverse customer base such that no single customer is more than 1% of revenue [14.4]. On scale, Cintas's c. $10.34 billion of revenue is roughly 4.3x UniFirst's and 3.8x Vestis's.

## Industry & business model

Uniform rental / facility services is a **route-based, recurring-revenue** business: garments and facility products are rented, laundered and delivered weekly from processing plants and branches over fixed local delivery routes. Both Cintas and UniFirst describe the markets as **local in nature and highly fragmented** [14.5], with low customer concentration on all sides [14.4][15.1].

Structurally, the U.S./Canada market is a **three-national-player oligopoly over a fragmented local-route base** — the public participants being Cintas, UniFirst and Vestis, alongside privately held Alsco and a long regional tail [7.14]. The competitive landscape was reshaped by the 2023 carve-out of **Vestis** from Aramark (Distribution Date September 30, 2023) [16]; Vestis calls itself the #2 provider in the U.S./Canada [15.2]. Vestis has since deteriorated: FY2025 revenue of c. $2.7 billion at only a 2.4% operating margin and a **net loss of $40.2 million** (a 291.8% swing from prior-year profit) [15.3][15.4], driven by rental revenue declines where lost business exceeded new business [15.5][15.4]. That a weakened #2 emerged just as Cintas moved on the #3-scale player materially shapes the "why now".

## Deal rationale & synergies — valuation and the price

The $310 price is rich against every independent yardstick in the record. The two financial advisors' standalone methodologies, and where the $310 lands:

| Methodology | Reference range (per share unless noted) | |
|---|---|---|
| J.P. Morgan — public trading multiples | 8.0x–10.0x EV/2026E adj. EBITDA | [8.5] |
| J.P. Morgan — selected transactions | 11.7x–14.2x FV/LTM EBITDA | [8.7] |
| J.P. Morgan — DCF | implied per-share equity range (7.50%–8.50% discount) | [8.8] |
| Goldman Sachs — DCF | 9.0% discount rate (CAPM cost of equity) | [8.9] |
| Goldman Sachs — selected precedents | 11.7x+ EV/LTM EBITDA (deals since 2015) | [8.10] |
| Goldman Sachs — premia paid (568 deals) | 21%–59% premium applied to $153.05 | [8.11] |

The comparable-company set used was Vestis and Elis SA [8.5]. Two grounded observations distilled from these anchors: the **$310 sits above the high end of every standalone methodology** from both banks, and the **c. 102.5% headline premium is nearly triple the 36% median** of Goldman's 568-deal premia-paid sample (102.5 / 36 ≈ 2.85x).

**On synergies:** Cintas asserts c. $375 million of run-rate operating cost synergies — spanning material cost, production, service and SG&A [1.2]. Two sizing facts frame their credibility: that figure is c. 2.03x UniFirst's entire FY2025 operating income, and the headline 8.0x EV/EBITDA multiple is computed **including** the synergies — backing them out raises the effective ex-synergy multiple materially. Cintas states the deal is accretive to EPS by the end of the second full year [1.5]. (Synergy size, accretion and "strategic fit" are the acquirer's own assertions and are scoped as such in the claims.)

## Financing & balance sheet

The cash leg is funded from cash on hand and committed credit, with **no financing condition** to closing [1.6][8.12]. Cintas secured fully committed bridge financing from Morgan Stanley, KeyBank and Wells Fargo [1.6] under a March 10, 2026 commitment letter [3.2]; after Tranche B was replaced by a new revolver, the remaining bridge commitment is $1.6 billion [3.2]. On March 27, 2026 Cintas entered a **$2.0 billion revolving credit facility** [19] carrying a leverage covenant (consolidated indebtedness / EBITDA) [19] and pricing of SOFR + 70–114 bps [19]. Before closing, Cintas expects to execute **c. $2.8 billion of permanent debt financing** [3.2], at an assumed 5% rate [3.3], taking on roughly $2.8 billion of additional indebtedness [3.4].

Cintas states net leverage at close will be c. 1.5x debt/EBITDA [1.7]. Three grounded reads on the structure: it is **structured conservatively for an investment-grade acquirer**, with the new debt sitting well inside the revolver's covenant headroom; **accretion is back-end-loaded and synergy-dependent**, expected only by end of year two after c. $2.8 billion of new debt; and **financing/liquidity close-risk is low but not zero** — the cash leg is not subject to a financing condition and is bridge-backstopped, but permanent financing was not yet secured at the proxy date [3.5].

The negotiation record is unusually informative on antitrust risk-sharing: UniFirst's advisor sought an "above market" reverse fee and a **"hell or high water"** regulatory-efforts covenant [8.25]; Cintas at one point terminated discussions over that covenant, the fee, and a "high 300s" price [8.25]; the parties settled on a **$350M reverse fee plus an obligation to litigate antitrust challenges** [8.25], with two four-month extensions available on a 10-month outside date [8.25]. The grounded synthesis: the agreement **shifts a defined slice of antitrust risk to Cintas but stops short of an unlimited hell-or-high-water commitment**, and because Cintas's closing obligation is not financing-conditioned yet specifically enforceable, Cintas bears financing-availability risk.

## Antitrust & regulatory — the dominant close-risk

The HSR timeline is the spine of the regulatory story: both parties filed on April 8, 2026 [8.1]; Cintas executed a **"pull-and-refile"**, withdrawing effective May 8 and refiling [8.1]; and at the refiled waiting period's expiry on **June 11, 2026 the FTC issued a Second Request** [8.1]. Closing also requires a foreign antitrust clearance and certain permit consents [8.26]. As of its June 12 approval, the company still expected an H2-2026 close [5].

On concentration: UniFirst's own 10-K names Cintas, Alsco and Vestis as its principal national competitors [7.15]. Trade analysis (labeled as such, not primary) estimates the combined entity at **45–50% of the North American market** [9] and frames the deal as a 3-to-2 combination of the "Big Three" [9]; Dealreporter reports the FTC interviewing competitors [20] and notes that finding divestiture buyers may be hard [20] given that Vestis — the natural national buyer — was downgraded by S&P to 'B' in December 2025 [20].

On the **base rate**, two directly comparable route-based deals cleared without structural relief: Cintas/G&K Services (2017) [21] and Aramark/AmeriPride, which received HSR early termination [22]. The grounded reads: the base rate for clearing such deals is favorable, but the realistic remedy path here skews toward **localized route/branch divestitures rather than a block — with elevated execution risk** precisely because the strongest national divestiture buyer is impaired.

## Management, governance & the activist

UniFirst is **family-controlled**: each Class B share carries 10 votes [8.27], giving the Croatti family over two-thirds of combined voting power [8.2]. Because the merger needs a two-thirds vote [8.28] and the family signed a voting-and-support agreement [8.29], approval was effectively assured from signing [8.29]. Governance is tightly held: Cynthia Croatti is the only family member on the board, and the General Counsel — who participated in board meetings — is her stepson [8.30]; CEO Steven Sintros personally led the Cintas contact [8.31].

**The chronology of the "flip."** Cintas first approached at $255/share in February 2022 [18.1]; the board publicly rejected a **$275/share all-cash** bid on January 7, 2025 [8.3]. **Engine Capital** (c. 3.2% of common) [10.2] then ran a proxy campaign asserting deeply disappointing total shareholder returns [10.1], an operating-margin decline from 12.8% (2019) to an expected 6.4% (FY2026) [10.3], and that rejecting $275 squandered value [10.4]; it nominated two directors [10.5] and won ISS support [23]. The board recommended against Engine [24] and **defeated its slate** at the December 15, 2025 meeting, keeping control intact [25]. Yet on February 8, 2026 the board agreed to **$310/share** [8.4] in a cash-and-stock structure [8.32], versus the earlier all-cash offers. The grounded read: the flip is best explained by **price and structure, not loss of control** — though vote certainty rests on a related-party / interested-controller dynamic.

**The bear case (Engine Capital, an interested party).** Engine argues that since founder-CEO Ron Croatti's 2017 death UniFirst has materially underperformed [10.6] on poor operational execution and capital allocation [10.7], that years of heavy investment have not paid off [10.7], and that the competitive gap with Cintas keeps widening [10.8]. All three leading proxy advisors (ISS, Glass Lewis, Egan-Jones) backed Engine's slate [26]. Notably, this is an **activist proxy contest, not a short-seller report** — no dedicated short thesis on UNF or CTAS was located.

## Operational profile & integration

This is operationally a **large route-network bolt-on**, not a merger of equals: Cintas runs c. 12,100 local delivery routes from 478 facilities with c. 22,900 vehicles [14.6], versus UniFirst's 281 facilities (c. 8.3M sq ft) [7.16], a c. 4,813-vehicle fleet (about one-fifth of Cintas's) [7.16], and c. 16,000 employees (about a third of Cintas's) [7.17]. Both are plant-and-branch route models [14.6]; route servicing is c. 83.8% of UniFirst's revenue, under 3-to-5-year contracts, so customer attrition during integration is a live risk [7.18][7.19].

The synergy mechanism Cintas asserts runs through combining processing capacity, route networks and supply chains and optimizing shared infrastructure [1.8][1.9]. Three grounded integration-risk facts temper it: the two firms run **structurally different supply chains** (UniFirst self-manufactures c. 62% of garments; Cintas largely outsources and runs five plants) [7.20][14.7]; UniFirst entered the deal with an **unremediated IT material weakness** narrowed to its CRM [7.21][7.22] and a multi-year ERP program running to 2027 with $45.3M capitalized [7.23]; and the proxy itself flags integration execution risk [2.3], non-recurring integration costs [2.4], and key-person/workforce retention risk [2.4].

## Litigation & legal

Litigation is a **clean, low tail-risk** for this deal. Both companies disclose only ordinary-course claims [7.24][14.8], and Cintas management opines its ordinary-course exposure is not material [14.8]. As of the DEFM14A date (May 11, 2026) there were **no lawsuits challenging the merger** and no securities-class or derivative suits filed [8.33], though the proxy notes such suits are commonly brought [8.33]; the shareholder vote then cleared on June 12, 2026 [4]. Two non-merger watch-items remain: UniFirst's unresolved **Mexican tax assessment**, still on appeal [7.25], and a Cintas reference to litigation "not considered in the ordinary course" whose size is not disclosed in the primaries [14.9]. The grounded read: litigation is low-risk with two contingent, non-merger watch-items.

## Supply chain & vertical integration

UniFirst is unusually **vertically integrated** for the sector: it self-manufactures c. 62% of the garments it places in service [7.26] from its own plants (San Luis Potosí, Mexico and Managua, Nicaragua, plus subcontractors) [7.27] and produces c. 99% of its floor mats at Cave City, Arkansas [7.27], citing better control of quality, price and speed for custom programs [7.26]. Cintas runs the **opposite model** — it sources finished products from many outside suppliers [14.10], operating only five of its own manufacturing facilities [14.10] alongside 12 distribution centers [14.11].

What the combination means for Cintas: it would acquire **in-house manufacturing capacity and a captive supply chain it has historically chosen not to own** — but also inherit UniFirst's Mexico/Nicaragua manufacturing footprint and its associated **tariff and imported-input exposure** [7.28], a sensitivity Cintas already flags through trade-agreement / geopolitical risk [14.12]. Both carry merchandise-in-service amortization (UniFirst over 6–36 months) as a real cost line [7.29][14.13]. Whether captive manufacturing is a strategic asset or an integration burden for Cintas's outsourced model is a judgment for the memo.

## How the cost synergies get realized — and whether the business supports it

The c. $375M is a **cost** figure, and UniFirst's own cost structure tells you where it must come from. UniFirst's cost of revenues was c. $1,542.4M — **63.4% of revenue** [7.30] — with merchandise amortization and production payroll as named components [7.30], plus SG&A of c. $565.1M (c. 23.2%) [7.31]; it runs a c. 4,813-vehicle delivery fleet [7.32]. Because the cost base is dominated by **route delivery, processing-plant labor, merchandise and overhead**, the realizable levers are the classic route-density ones — Cintas frames the synergy as "integrating complementary processing capacity, route networks, service infrastructure, supply chains" [1.10] and leveraging the combined route networks [27]: overlapping routes consolidated into more stops per truck, processing plants and branches utilized harder, procurement scaled, and duplicate public-company overhead removed. Critically, the filings do **not** disclose a dollar split of route vs. plant vs. merchandise cost, so the precise mechanism is a *reasoned* judgment for the memo, not a cited figure.

**Base rate — does Cintas execute laundry-consolidation synergies?** Its 2016 G&K Services acquisition targeted c. $130–140M of synergies over four years [28]; integration spend wound down to c. $7.8M by FY2019 [29], a pattern consistent with realized synergy, though Cintas never published an explicit synergy-realization reconciliation. For scale, the $375M target is c. 2.03x UniFirst's FY2025 operating income and is embedded in the 8.0x headline multiple [1.10].

## Why Cintas is so persistent

Cintas pursued UniFirst for **nearly four years across at least five distinct approaches**: a $255/share indication in February 2022 [8.34], a $275 proposal in November 2024 [8.35] reiterated repeatedly [8.35], made **public** in January 2025 [8.36], formally terminated in March 2025 [8.37], then **re-submitted** in December 2025 during the Engine Capital proxy fight [8.38] before the February 2026 agreement at $310 [8.39]. Cintas frames the rationale as combining complementary route networks [1.11] to serve c. 1.5 million customers [1.11] and "better compete in an increasingly competitive environment" [18.3], and characterized completing the transaction as a top priority [18.3].

A structural fact underpins the persistence: UniFirst is one of only **three scaled national players** named in the filings (Cintas, Alsco, Vestis) [7.33] in a highly competitive industry [7.33] — i.e. the last sizeable, independent, family-controlled operator available to consolidate. Whether that makes the pursuit offensive lead-extension or defensive consolidation (before a weakened Vestis or a financial buyer takes the asset) is the memo's call.

## Customer base: why only ~4 wearers per customer

The deck's penetration framing — a combined **<8M uniform "wearers" across <2M customers** (c. 4 per customer), in a 180M+ worker / 16M business market — looks oddly low, but the verified facts explain it. Cintas serves **"over one million businesses of all types, from small service and manufacturing companies to major corporations that employ thousands of people"** [14.4], and UniFirst likewise serves "businesses of all sizes across multiple industries and sectors" [7.34] — a base **dominated by small businesses by count**, each with only a handful of uniformed staff. And uniforms are only part of the offering: c. **95% of Cintas's revenue is route servicing across Uniform Rental & Facility Services, First Aid & Safety, and Fire Protection** [14.14] — so a large share of accounts buy floor mats, restroom supplies, first-aid cabinets or fire protection with **few or no rented-uniform wearers at all**. The base is therefore **broad and shallow** — millions of small, mixed-service stops rather than a few large uniformed accounts.

(The <8M / <2M figures themselves are from the image-PDF investor deck — management framing, not machine-verified.) This same structural feature is *why* route density is the core synergy lever: a business made of millions of small, dispersed stops is exactly what consolidating overlapping routes optimizes.

## Preliminary read & coverage

**What the facts establish (for the IC memo to weigh).** This is a strategically coherent #1-buys-#3 route-density consolidation at a **full price**: $310 is above every standalone valuation anchor and the premium is near-triple the precedent median, so the economics lean heavily on Cintas realizing c. $375M of cost synergies that are themselves c. 2x the target's operating income. Deal *certainty on the vote* is structurally high (family lock-up) [8.29], but *certainty on closing* hinges on the **FTC Second Request** [8.1] in a 3-to-2 market [9] where the natural divestiture buyer is impaired [20] — partly mitigated by the $350M reverse fee and litigate-the-challenge covenant [8.25]. The "good deal vs. will-it-close" questions should be argued separately.

**Coverage.** 10 of 12 checklist areas are covered with verified claims; the two not searched — macro/geopolitical and ESG/environmental — were the optional tail outside the approved scope, and the coverage map reports **0 required/recommended gaps**. No covered area was flagged thin.

**Honest limitations from the research pass:**
- **Synergy lever / cost-vs-revenue split:** the c. $375M is grounded as Cintas's assertion with its broad buckets, but UniFirst's cost composition is now grounded (cost of revenues c. 63.4% of revenue; SG&A c. 23.2%), and the Dec 2025 investor deck was read manually (it is an image-PDF, so not machine-verifiable through the spine). But the filings still do **not** disclose a dollar split of route vs. plant vs. merchandise cost, so the precise per-stop density mechanism remains a *reasoned judgment* for the memo, not a cited figure.
- **Market size / share:** no primary filing gives a U.S. TAM; the 45–50% combined-share and "c. $20B market" figures are **trade-press/analyst estimates, labeled as such**, corroborated only against each filer's competitor list.
- **Exact MAE carve-outs, matching-rights day-count, and the calendar outside date** did not extract cleanly from the agreement's glossary/definitions and are residual gaps.
- **Quarantines (14 of 262 proposals):** all either duplicates of facts verified elsewhere (e.g. the $350M reverse fee / hell-or-high-water record, verified under the merger-agreement sources) or non-load-bearing details (a Cintas rental-margin datapoint, the G&K realized-synergy reconciliation); no load-bearing fact was lost.
