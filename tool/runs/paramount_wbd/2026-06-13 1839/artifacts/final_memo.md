# Final IC Memo — Warner Bros. Discovery (Paramount Skydance / WBD)

*Paramount Skydance's $31.00/share all-cash acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery — final IC memo, post red-team*

## 1. Executive summary

On February 27, 2026, Paramount Skydance (NASDAQ: PSKY) signed a definitive agreement to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery (NASDAQ: WBD) for **$31.00 per share in cash**, valuing WBD at c. **$81 billion of equity** and c. **$110 billion of enterprise value** [1.1][1.2]. This is a **horizontal consolidation** of two sub-scale streaming/studio groups, reached only after a contested auction: WBD had agreed to sell its studios-and-streaming business to **Netflix** at $27.75/share before Paramount went hostile, escalated to an all-cash whole-company bid, and was declared the 'Superior Proposal' on February 26, 2026 [2.1][3.1]. Paramount paid WBD's **$2.8B Netflix termination fee** to clear the prior deal [3.1].

The strategic logic is real but the buyer is reaching. Paramount+ is sub-scale and loss-making; WBD brings a profitable c. 132M-subscriber HBO Max and a 15,000-title Warner library [4.1][5.1][1.3].

> **Our view —** The deal's operating lever is content-cost density — spreading combined content spend over a c. 200M-subscriber base — which is genuine but partial: audience overlap means net reach is below the additive total, and even combined the base is only c. 0.65x Netflix's. The strategic rationale (gain scale or retreat) is genuine, but the full price and junk-rated leverage show a buyer reaching.

**What dominates the file:**

- **Price.** $31.00 is a **147% premium** to the $12.54 unaffected price [6] and sits at the very top of the advisors' DCF range ($21.16-$31.91) [3.2] — full value, won in an auction.
- **Leverage.** c. $54-57.5B of new debt takes leverage to **6-6.5x** and total debt to **$79-87B**; S&P cuts the company to junk at close [7][8]. This is the central financial risk.
- **Synergies.** Management sized **c. $6B** of cost synergies (after disclosing none at announcement) — material and mostly tech/overhead, though its size relative to peers is ambiguous (c. 2-3x Netflix's $2-3B but well below a >$16B figure Netflix's co-CEO implied).
- **The melting cash cow.** The linear networks Paramount is levering up to buy are declining double digits — the dominant deterioration risk.

> **Our view on close —** The deal is **likely to close, but with genuine residual non-federal gating risk** — not a near-certainty. The two biggest gates (the binding shareholder vote and federal antitrust) are satisfied and financing is Ellison-backstopped, but **three** channels remain open — a multi-state AG coalition, the FCC foreign-ownership waiver, and an open UK CMA probe — and the base rate that supports closing rests on only two precedents (AT&T-Time Warner, Disney-Fox), neither involving a state-AG coalition or a foreign-ownership waiver. Buyer determination is an effort, not a clearance probability.

## 2. Company overview

**Warner Bros. Discovery (target).** WBD operates three segments — **Streaming** (HBO Max, discovery+, HBO), **Studios** (Warner Bros. film/TV), and **Global Linear Networks** (U.S. and international cable networks, including CNN) [5.2]. FY2025 revenue was **$37,296M, down 5%**, extending a multi-year decline from $41,321M in FY2023 [5.3]; total Adjusted EBITDA was **$8,744M, down 3%** [5.4]. It serves a global footprint — c. **35,500 employees** split 48% U.S. / 52% international [5.5] — and ended 2025 with **131.6M streaming subscribers** (+14.7M in the year) as HBO Max expanded past 100 markets [5.1]. The shape of the business is the key fact:


| WBD segment (FY2025)           | Revenue    | % of total | Adj. EBITDA        | YoY EBITDA |              |
| ------------------------------ | ---------- | ---------- | ------------------ | ---------- | ------------ |
| Global Linear Networks         | $17,656M   | c. 47%     | $6,412M            | −21%       | [5.6][5.7]   |
| Streaming                      | $12,619M   | c. 34%     | +$693M improvement | —          | [5.8][5.9]   |
| Studios                        | $10,876M   | c. 29%     | $2,545M            | +54%       | [5.10][5.9]  |
| *Streaming & Studios combined* | *$20,371M* | —          | *$3,496M*          | *+64%*     | [5.11][5.12] |


The declining linear networks remain the **largest revenue and cash contributor** while streaming/studios is the smaller, growing engine. WBD's own strategy treats linear as a business to **'manage,'** not grow [5.13], and it had a board-approved plan to split into two companies before Paramount intervened [5.14]. Credit quality was already eroding (2025 downgrades from all three agencies) [5.15], and WBD paid **$2,295M of cash interest** in 2025 [5.16].

**Paramount Skydance (acquirer).** Formed from the 2025 Skydance/Paramount Global merger, Paramount guided to c. **$30B of revenue** [9.1] and shows the **same linear-decline / streaming-growth split** as its target — Q3 2025 TV Media revenue −12%, Direct-to-Consumer +17% [9.2]. **Paramount+ had c. 79.1M subscribers** (+13%) [9.3] but its streaming arm was still **loss-making** ($158M quarterly loss) [4.1]. Its stated strategy is explicitly to build a global, scaled streaming platform' [9.4] — the thesis that drives this deal.

## 3. Industry & market

The deal sits at the center of the **streaming endgame**. The global video-streaming market was c. **$160B in 2025** (growing c. 18.5%/yr) [10], but it is top-heavy and the business is a fixed-cost game.

> **Our view —** The profit engine is subscriber scale against a largely fixed content cost: Netflix's 325M base amortizes content over far more subscribers (c. $2.4B quarterly profit) than sub-scale, loss-making Paramount+, so the binding constraint on streaming profitability is reaching the scale where content cost per subscriber falls below ARPU — the structural reason a sub-scale operator must gain scale or retreat.


| Streamer (latest disclosed) | Subscribers     | Latest-quarter streaming profit |            |
| --------------------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------- | ---------- |
| Netflix                     | 325M            | +$2.42B (rev +17.6%)            | [4.2][4.3] |
| Disney+ / Hulu (+ ESPN+)    | 195.7M (+24.1M) | +$450M                          | [4.4][4.5] |
| WBD (HBO Max)               | c. 132M         | +$393M (FY +$1.37B)             | [4.6][4.7] |
| Paramount+                  | c. 79M          | −$158M                          | [4.8][4.1] |
| Peacock (Comcast)           | 44M             | unprofitable                    | [4.9]      |


Linear, meanwhile, is in structural decline: U.S. pay-TV penetration has fallen from c. 80% (2018) to c. 50%, with households down c. 6% YoY [11]. Mature, declining industries consolidate — the backdrop to this deal. Sector M&A over the last decade has generally **closed**, with horizontal overlap remedied by divestiture rather than blocked:


| Acquirer / target         | Year     | Value     | Rationale                         | Regulatory / outcome                                    |              |
| ------------------------- | -------- | --------- | --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ------------ |
| AT&T / Time Warner        | 2018-19  | $85.4B    | vertical (distribution + content) | DOJ sued to block, **lost on appeal**, no conditions    | [12.1][12.2] |
| Disney / 21st Century Fox | 2019     | c. $71B   | content scale                     | cleared with **22 regional sports network divestiture** | [13][14]     |
| Discovery / WarnerMedia   | 2022     | c. $43B   | scale (RMT) — **created WBD**     | completed                                               | [15.1][15.2] |
| Comcast / Sky             | 2018     | c. $39B   | international scale               | won a three-round auction                               | [16]         |
| Netflix / Warner Bros.    | Dec 2025 | c. $82.7B | studios + streaming               | **abandoned** — bid Paramount beat                      | [17]         |


> **Our view —** This precedent set establishes a base rate in which the binding antitrust constraint has been horizontal overlap remedied by divestiture (Disney's ESPN + RSNs), not vertical-integration blocks (AT&T) — a base rate that favors closing for the Paramount-WBD review.

## 4. Deal structure & protections

An all-cash acquisition of 100% of WBD at **$31.00/share** (c. $81B equity / c. $110B EV) [1.1][1.2], funded with c. **$54-57.5B of new debt** led by Bank of America and Citigroup, with **no financing condition** [7][3.3]. The shareholder vote is **done** (approved, April 2026) [18] and DOJ antitrust is **cleared** [19]; the outside date is **March 4, 2027** with one automatic 3-month extension [3.4]. The risk-allocation terms are markedly **seller-favorable**:


| Provision                            | Term                                                         |             |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------- |
| Company termination fee (WBD → PSKY) | $3 billion                                                   | [3.5]       |
| Reverse regulatory fee (PSKY → WBD)  | **$7 billion**                                               | [3.6]       |
| Netflix fee pre-paid by PSKY         | $2.8 billion (recoverable only on a WBD-side termination)    | [3.7][3.6]  |
| Ticking fee                          | $0.25/share per quarter after Sept 30, 2026                  | [3.8]       |
| Regulatory-efforts standard          | reasonable best efforts, **capped — not hell-or-high-water** | [3.9][3.10] |
| Financing condition                  | none                                                         | [3.3]       |
| Specific performance                 | available to WBD                                             | [3.11]      |
| Sponsor backstop                     | $43.3B Ellison guarantee                                     | [20]        |
| MAC/MAE                              | no Company MAE since Sept 30, 2025 represented               | [3.12]      |


> **Our view —** The package is markedly seller-favorable and signals unusually high buyer determination: the $7B reverse fee is c. 2.3x WBD's $3B fee, Paramount fronted the $2.8B Netflix fee, waived any financing condition, and granted specific performance — shifting regulatory and timing risk onto the buyer. The one limit is that the efforts covenant is capped rather than hell-or-high-water, so the $7B fee — not an unlimited obligation to divest or litigate — is the antitrust backstop. That cap is moot for the **federal** channel that already cleared, but it remains **live** for the unresolved state-AG and FCC channels: if either sought conditions, Paramount is not obliged to accept divestitures or remedies amounting to a material adverse effect — so the cap is relevant precisely where close risk now sits, the reverse of 'largely moot.'

## 5. Strategic & financial rationale

**Strategic logic — the lever, then the label.** Paramount is sub-scale and loss-making in streaming; the combination stacks Paramount+ (c. 79M, loss-making) onto HBO Max (c. 132M, profitable) to reach a c. 200M-subscriber base, plus a 15,000-title combined library [4.8][4.6][4.10][1.3].

> **Our view —** The operating lever is **content-cost density** — spreading combined content and platform spend over a far larger subscriber base — which makes this a **horizontal scale consolidation** (the label follows from the lever, not the reverse). But the lever is partial: audience overlap (7.6M dual subscribers; 86.7% viewing affinity) means net reach is below the additive c. 211M, and even combined the base is only c. 0.65x Netflix's 325M, so the merger narrows rather than closes the scale gap. It is worth being precise about the frontier: even combined, this is **not the scale leader** — Amazon Prime Video leads the U.S. market (22%) with Netflix second (21%) — and because Paramount's own streaming arm enters loss-making, stacking it onto profitable HBO Max is not cleanly accretive density.
>
> On the choice to take the whole company: an all-cash whole-company bid was what it took to beat Netflix, and Global Linear Networks' $6,412M EBITDA (c. 1.8x the Streaming & Studios division) is the near-term cash engine to help service the debt. The board's flip itself delivered both higher headline value (+11.7% over Netflix's $27.75) and cash certainty over a part-stock alternative. But 'Paramount **wants** the linear cash flow' overstates intent: that EBITDA is already declining 21% a year and Paramount itself doubted the value of WBD's cable assets, so the more defensible read is that it is **partly stuck absorbing a fast-eroding asset** to win the studio/streaming jewels — and the cash engine it is counting on for debt service is the fastest-shrinking part of the target.

**Financial rationale — the price against value.** The deal anchor:


|                                            | Per share                                     |        |
| ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------- | ------ |
| Offer price                                | $31.00 (all cash)                             | [1.1]  |
| Unaffected price (Sept 10, 2025)           | $12.54                                        | [6]    |
| Implied premium                            | **147%** ( 31.00 / 12.54 = 2.47x, i.e. +147%) | [6]    |
| Last pre-announcement price (Feb 26, 2026) | $28.80 (already lifted by the auction)        | [3.13] |


Against intrinsic value, the one fully grounded framework in the record is the financial advisors' DCF:


| Method (source)                                          | Assumption                                          | Implied per-share range | Where $31.00 lands                     |       |
| -------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------- | -------------------------------------- | ----- |
| Allen & Co. / J.P. Morgan — sum-of-parts DCF [3.14][3.2] | discount rates 6.75%-9.5% across the two businesses | **$21.16 - $31.91**     | **at the top** ($31.00 vs $31.91 high) | [3.2] |


The proxy also references comparable-company and precedent-transaction analyses and J.P. Morgan's own DCF discount rates [3.15], but those did not surface as verifiable per-share ranges in the research pass, so they are flagged in Limits rather than asserted.

> **Our view —** The 147% premium is best read as an **auction clearing price** — the stock had already run to $28.80 on the Netflix deal, and the price reflects a contested process — but auction-cleared and **overpaid are not mutually exclusive**. $31.00 sits roughly 99% of the way up the advisors' DCF range ($21.16-$31.91), i.e. the top of intrinsic value, and Netflix — the marginal informed bidder — walked at $31 as 'no longer financially attractive,' which is evidence the price exceeds value as much as it is evidence of a clearing outcome. The honest characterization is **both an auction result and a full-to-stretched price**, with no standalone-value cushion.

**Accretion / dilution.** Paramount disclosed **no** accretion guidance (unlike Netflix, which guided to GAAP-EPS accretion by year two) [1.2][2.2].

> **Our view —** Funded all-cash with c. $54-57.5B of mostly junk-rated new debt, the deal is **likely dilutive to free cash flow near-term**, turning accretive only as the phased c. $6B of synergies (c. 30% year one, 70% by year two) and linear cash flow build — accretion is back-end-loaded and synergy-dependent.

**Synergy credibility.** Management's deal announcement sized **no** synergy figure, presenting valuation only 'on a fully synergized basis' [1.2]; it later quantified **c. $6B** of cost synergies, c. 30% in year one and 70% by year two [21], with Ellison reaffirming the $6B and rebutting a larger implied number [22].

> **Our view —** Treat the $6B as a management assertion to size, not accept. It is **material but its size relative to peers is genuinely ambiguous**, and the benchmarks cut both ways: it is c. two-to-three times the $2-3B Netflix disclosed, but Netflix was buying only the smaller Studios+Streaming scope, while Netflix's co-CEO separately implied synergies in excess of $16B — against which $6B looks conservative. The 'c. 69% of WBD's $8,744M standalone EBITDA' frame also overstates the stretch, since the synergies are extracted across the **combined** Paramount+WBD overhead and tech, not WBD alone. What is not ambiguous is that the $6B is sourced mainly from streaming-tech and overhead consolidation Paramount has only begun internally — credible as a **cost-led** (more realizable) target, but unproven at this scale. And because the 7.5x purchase multiple and 4.3x leverage are struck **on a fully synergized basis** with no disclosed synergy dollar split, the synergies-vs-premium test cannot be fully checked from disclosure — the reported economics already bake in the unrealized $6B, and on a pre-synergy basis both the multiple and the leverage are higher, so the synergy assumption is doing real work in making the price and balance sheet look serviceable.

**Deal-economics risks.** The synergy runs through a **streaming-platform and back-office integration** — combining Paramount+ and HBO Max into one app (pricing and name still undisclosed) [23].

> **Our view —** The bulk of the $6B runs through technology and overhead consolidation rather than content or headcount cuts, making the thesis dependent on executing an integration only begun internally; and combining the two apps carries **subscriber-attrition risk** because the c. 200M figure overstates net reach given heavy overlap — deduplication could shed double-paying subscribers and churn dissatisfied users. There is also a credibility tension in the cost line: management frames 'no layoffs' yet concedes corporate overhead 'will absolutely have to' be rationalized, with both companies having already absorbed prior layoff waves — leaving open how much non-content overhead remains to cut.

## 6. Tail risks

Ranked by what could **derail or devalue** the deal. Note that several standard tail-risk areas — the activist/contest and the governance controversy — do **not** threaten closing here, and are explained as such below rather than ranked as risks.

**1. Financing & leverage (highest).** c. $54-57.5B of new debt takes pro-forma leverage to **6-6.5x** and total debt to **$79-87B**; S&P will cut Paramount to junk ('BB') at close and does not expect leverage below its 4.25x threshold until **2029** [7][8].

> **Our view —** This is the central financial risk: the deleveraging plan to 3.0x by FY2029 rests on synergy execution against a structurally declining base — it requires realizing c. $6B of synergies on top of $79-87B of debt while the largest acquired segment is in secular decline, the precise risk S&P cites in keeping leverage above 4.25x until 2029. The rating agency itself does not take the plan at face value: S&P read Ellison's leverage pledge as a **tacit promise to inject capital if needed**, pushing to make it public after skeptics called the leverage 'frightening' — so the plan implicitly leans on a sponsor backstop, and the refinancing task is the larger c. $32.7B standalone WBD schedule (c. $16.5B in 2027) into already-scarce leveraged-loan supply; a one-to-two-turn EBITDA erosion from the linear melt would push the sub-4.25x path further out. Liquidity is supported by a $5B revolver and WBD's c. $4.6B cash.

**2. Deterioration of the target's fundamentals (linear melt).** WBD's FY2025 domestic linear advertising fell **11% on 25% audience declines** and distribution fell 8% on a 9% subscriber drop [5.17][5.18]; pay-TV penetration has fallen from c. 80% to c. 50% [11].

> **Our view —** This is the deterioration risk that most threatens the thesis: Global Linear Networks is the near-term cash engine meant to help service the debt, so if it melts faster than planned the deleveraging math breaks — the linear-melt risk and the leverage risk are the same risk viewed from two ends.

**3. Overpayment (value risk).** $31.00 is a 147% premium at the top of the DCF range, and competing bidder Netflix walked at that price [6][3.2][24].

> **Our view —** This is a value risk, not a close risk: Paramount paid full intrinsic value plus an auction premium, so the equity returns depend on synergy capture rather than buying at a discount.

**4. State attorneys-general litigation (the principal close risk).** Despite DOJ clearance, c. 10 states are reportedly drafting a complaint, California confirms an open investigation, and state AGs retain independent authority [25][26][27]; the litigation is process-/deal-driven otherwise.

> **Our view —** This is the largest **remaining** close risk, and it is **genuinely uncertain rather than clearly low-probability**. The sector base rate that favors closing (AT&T-Time Warner upheld over a DOJ court loss; Disney-Fox cleared via divestiture) is built from **federal** DOJ actions and does not speak to a coordinated multi-state coalition challenging on a 'federal enforcers failed to act' political theory, for which the fact base offers no comparable precedent. So while a state suit rarely enjoins a DOJ-cleared deal, the honest read is that the relevant base rate does not exist. Whether this clearance is durable also depends on **why** the DOJ cleared: it stated the deal would *increase* competition (a permissive streaming-era market read), but the political alignment of the Ellison/Oracle ownership is the competing explanation — and a clearance on the merits is harder for states to dislodge than a politically-eased one.

**5. FCC foreign-ownership / political review.** Total foreign ownership is **49.5%** (38.5% from Saudi, Qatari and Abu Dhabi funds), requiring an FCC waiver above 25%; Senate Democrats and the FCC's lone Democrat have objected on foreign-influence grounds [28.1][28.2][28.3].

> **Our view —** A genuine pending gating item. The mitigant Paramount offers — the Ellison family's retained voting control and the investors' asserted passivity — is the **acquirer's own assertion**, and the objection (Senate Democrats, Commissioner Gomez) is precisely that even passive foreign-government-linked funds gain data and influence access; so 'Ellison control resolves it' assumes the regulator accepts passivity, which is what is contested. It is more likely a **delay than a block** risk, but it is not the near-formality the count of 'two open items' implied. The political alignment cuts both ways: Ellison/Oracle ties plausibly eased the federal clearance even as they fuel these objections and the post-close editorial-independence question.

There is also a **third open jurisdiction**: the UK CMA formally opened a probe around June 9, 2026 that remained pending even as Australia and a dozen-plus jurisdictions cleared. [29]

**6. Integration / execution.** The c. $6B synergy and the c. 200M-subscriber claim both run through a not-yet-started app and back-office integration with heavy subscriber overlap [23][30][31].

> **Our view —** Execution risk is real but bears on value, not close: it threatens synergy capture (and therefore the deleveraging path) rather than completion.

**7. Litigation (low).** Beyond the state-AG threat, the deal drew only process suits — Paramount's own Chancery complaint (expedition denied), a 14D-9 disclosure suit, the Nicosia merger-objection suit and 15 demand letters — plus WBD's standing pre-merger securities case [5.19][32.1][32.2][5.20].

> **Our view —** Low close-risk: these disclosure/process claims typically settle via supplemental disclosure and cannot block the deal.

**Why two standard areas are NOT ranked risks:**

- **Activist / contested-takeover dynamic — now favors the deal.** The leverage bear case ('largest LBO in history,' c. 7x EBITDA before synergies) came from the WBD board while it opposed the bid; that board has since flipped and shareholders have approved, so the contest pressure is resolved in the deal's favor. [33]

> **Our view —** The shareholder-pressure dynamic here was a two-sided contested-solicitation / hostile-tender fight, not an activist short campaign, and it has been resolved in the deal's favor by the board's flip and the shareholder vote. But separate **'contest resolved' (true) from 'leverage thesis resolved' (false)**: the flip retired the proxy fight, not the economics — post-financing the leverage is larger, not smaller, and the board flipped under a $7B reverse-fee / specific-performance / fiduciary-out structure and alongside a failed advisory pay vote, a last-minute tax gross-up, and a $114M insider sale, so it is **not a clean validation of the deal's economics**. The leverage/financeability case (corroborated directionally by Netflix walking at $31 and by Paramount itself doubting the cable assets' value) survives as a **value risk**, not a close risk.

- **Governance controversy — bears on stewardship, not closing.** Control concentrates in David Ellison [34] and Zaslav's c. $887M single-trigger parachute survived a failed advisory vote [3.16][18].

> **Our view —** These are post-close stewardship and CNN/CBS-News editorial-independence concerns, not close risks: the only binding shareholder vote already passed. They do, however, concentrate **key-person risk** in one controlling individual — Ellison holds 100% of family voting interests, chairs and runs the company, controls the NAI board, and personally fronts the editorial-independence pledges — so governance and the CNN/CBS-News reputational questions hinge on him rather than dispersed shareholder oversight.

## 7. Assessment & what would change our view

**(a) Scope & limits.** This assessment covers the deal's **strategic & financial rationale, its tail risks, and how likely it is to close**. It is **not** a detailed merger-agreement/legal review, a tax analysis, or an audit of management's projections; any figure not fully sourced is flagged as such here and in Limits.

**(b) Will it close? — Likely, but not a near-certainty.** Weigh the forces.

*Pushing the deal to completion:* the binding **shareholder vote is already done** [18]; **federal antitrust is cleared with no conditions**; **financing is committed and Ellison-backstopped** ($43.3B guarantee, no financing condition) [20][3.3]; and the buyer is **highly determined** ($7B reverse fee, pre-paid Netflix fee, specific performance). A buyer this far over-committed will push hard to complete.

*Pulling it apart — three open channels, not two:* a **threatened multi-state AG suit** on a 'federal enforcers failed to act' theory [25], a **pending FCC foreign-ownership waiver** (49.5% foreign / 38.5% Gulf-fund) [28.1], and an **open UK CMA probe** [29]. None is a likely blocker, but the base rate that reassures on the first two is built from federal DOJ precedents that do not fit a state coalition or a foreign-ownership review.

> **Our view —** The deal is **likely to close, but with genuine residual non-federal gating risk** — not the near-certainty a 'two satisfied gates' reading suggests. The two biggest forces (the vote and federal antitrust) are satisfied and financing is locked, but three channels remain open and the supporting base rate rests on only two precedents (n=2), neither involving a state-AG coalition or a foreign-ownership waiver. Buyer determination is an effort, not a clearance probability — it cannot defeat an injunction or an FCC denial. The leverage, overpayment and integration concerns bear on whether the deal **creates value**, not on whether it **completes**.

**(c) What to watch — and what would change this view:**

- **A filed state-AG complaint seeking a preliminary injunction.** A multi-state suit that actually moves to enjoin (not merely 'investigate') would be the one development that could delay or block closing — watch California and the c. 10-state coalition [25][26]. This is the monitorable most likely to flip the close verdict.
- **The FCC foreign-ownership ruling.** Denial or onerous conditions on the 49.5%/38.5% foreign-ownership waiver would push timing out toward the March 4, 2027 outside date [28.1][3.4].
- **The UK CMA review.** Escalation of the open CMA probe to an in-depth (Phase 2) review would add a third foreign-jurisdiction gate and pressure the timeline [29].
- **A faster linear melt or a credit-market shift.** Linear declines beyond the current double-digit pace, or a junk-market dislocation, would not stop closing but would worsen the post-close deleveraging and the value case.
- **Synergy evidence.** Any disclosure that sizes or sources the c. $6B differently — or that the app integration slips — would move the value (not close) read.

## 8. Limitations

- **Synergy basis.** The c. $6B is grounded as management's assertion with a 30%/70% phasing [21], but the filings give **no cost-vs-revenue split and no dollar breakdown** by technology vs. overhead — so the precise mechanism is a reasoned judgment, not a cited figure.
- **Synergy break-even not precisely computed.** The Plan asked what annual synergy level would offset the premium and the incremental interest on c. $54-57.5B of new debt [7]; we could not compute a falsifiable break-even without a grounded blended interest rate (one was not disclosed in the verified record). Qualitatively, the c. $6B run-rate synergy is of the same order of magnitude as the likely incremental interest, so the deal's free-cash-flow accretion hinges on near-full synergy capture — but the precise crossover year is not established here.
- **Close-verdict base rate is thin.** The judgment that the deal is likely to close leans on a media-M&A base rate built from only two precedents (AT&T-Time Warner, Disney-Fox), neither of which involved a multi-state AG coalition or a foreign-ownership review — so the close probability is a reasoned weighting, not a base-rate-backed estimate, for the state-AG and FCC channels specifically.
- **Valuation frameworks.** Only the advisors' sum-of-parts **DCF range ($21.16-$31.91) is fully grounded** [3.2]; the comparable-company and precedent-transaction ranges and J.P. Morgan's own DCF range referenced in the proxy did not surface as verifiable per-share ranges in the research pass, so the price-vs-value read leans on the one DCF range plus the premium.
- **Pro-forma leverage** is reported at **6-6.5x by independent sources** [7] versus a c. 4.3x management framing expressed 'on a fully synergized basis'; the management 4.3x figure and its reconciliation **quarantined** on sourcing and are not relied on here (the point survives via S&P's verified 'not below 4.25x until 2029') [8].
- **FCC status.** An earlier 2025 FCC broadcast-license approval (a 2-1 vote with a dissent) related to the **Skydance/CBS** transaction, **not** this WBD deal; for the WBD deal the FCC foreign-ownership review was **still pending** as of June 2026 and is treated as unresolved [28.1][28.3].
- **Scope.** ESG/environmental was deliberately out of scope (immaterial for a media combination; the live social/governance risks are captured under governance and macro). Coverage otherwise reported 0 required/recommended gaps.
