# Research Brief — Paramount Skydance's Acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery

_Stage-3 grounded research brief — every load-bearing fact cites a verified, source-checked claim. Analytical judgments are deferred to the IC memo._

## Executive summary

On February 27, 2026, Paramount Skydance Corporation (NASDAQ: PSKY) signed a definitive merger agreement to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery, Inc. (NASDAQ: WBD) for **$31.00 per share in cash**, valuing WBD at roughly **$81 billion in equity value and $110 billion in enterprise value** [1.1][1.2]. WBD shareholders approved the merger at an April 2026 special meeting [2]; on **June 12, 2026 the U.S. Department of Justice cleared the deal with no divestitures and no conditions** [3][4]. The parties target a close around Q3 2026, with a contractual outside date of **March 4, 2027** [5.1].

This is the largest media combination of the decade, and it arrived only after a **contested, multi-bidder auction**. WBD had agreed in December 2025 to sell its studios-and-streaming business to **Netflix** at $27.75/share (≈$82.7B enterprise value), spinning off its linear networks [6.1]. Paramount went **hostile**, took an all-cash tender offer directly to shareholders [7], and escalated until the WBD board flipped, declaring Paramount's $31.00 all-cash bid a "Company Superior Proposal" on February 26, 2026 [5.2]; Paramount paid WBD's **$2.8 billion termination fee to Netflix** to clear the prior deal [5.2][5.3].

Four threads dominate the file and frame the work for the IC memo:
- **Price.** $31.00 cash is a **147% premium** to WBD's unaffected price of $12.54 (Sept 10, 2025) [8] and sits **at the very top of the financial advisors' DCF range** ($21.16–$31.91) [5.4].
- **Leverage.** The all-cash deal is funded with roughly **$54–57.5 billion of new debt**, taking pro-forma leverage to ~**6–6.5x EBITDA** and total debt to ~**$79–87 billion** — financed against a target whose largest segment is in secular decline [9]. S&P will cut the combined company to junk ('BB') at close [10].
- **Synergies.** Paramount's deal announcement sized **no** synergy figure, expressing valuation only "on a fully synergized basis" [1.2]; management subsequently quantified **"over $6 billion"** of cost synergies, phased c. 30% in year one and 70% by year two [11][12].
- **Politics & regulation.** The DOJ cleared the deal unconditionally, but a **threatened multi-state attorneys-general lawsuit** [13.1][14], an unresolved **FCC foreign-ownership review** (49.5% foreign ownership, 38.5% from Gulf state funds) [15.1], and an **editorial-independence controversy** over common ownership of CNN and CBS News [13.2] are the residual overhang.

A condition of Paramount's obligation to close is that WBD has **not** completed its previously planned separation into two companies [17.1].

## The companies

**Warner Bros. Discovery (the target).** WBD reports three segments — **Streaming** (HBO Max, discovery+, HBO premium pay-TV), **Studios** (Warner Bros. film/TV production), and **Global Linear Networks** (U.S. and international cable networks, including CNN) [17.2]. FY2025 consolidated revenue was **$37,296 million, down 5%**, continuing a multi-year decline from $41,321M in FY2023 [17.3]; total Adjusted EBITDA was **$8,744 million, down 3%** [17.4]. The shape of the business is the central fact: the **declining linear networks are still the largest revenue and cash contributor**, while the growing streaming/studios businesses are smaller but rising.

| WBD segment (FY2025) | Revenue | YoY | Adj. EBITDA | YoY | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Global Linear Networks | $17,656M (c. 47%) | −12% | $6,412M | −21% | [17.5][17.6] |
| Streaming | $12,619M (c. 34%) | +9% | +$693M improvement | — | [17.7][17.8] |
| Studios | $10,876M (c. 29%) | +5% | $2,545M | +54% | [17.9][17.8] |
| *Streaming & Studios combined* | *$20,371M* | *+3%* | *$3,496M* | *+64%* | [17.10][17.11] |

On the eve of the deal, the "melting" Global Linear Networks segment still produced roughly **1.8x the EBITDA** of the growth-engine Streaming & Studios division ($6,412M / $3,496M ≈ 1.83x). WBD ended 2025 with **131.6 million streaming subscribers** (+14.7M in the year) [17.12] and c. 35,500 employees [17.13]. Its stated strategy is to grow streaming globally, enhance Studios, and **"manage" the linear networks** — explicitly treating linear as a business to be managed, not grown [17.14] — and it had a board-approved plan to split into two companies before Paramount intervened [17.1]. Credit-quality was already eroding: S&P, Moody's and Fitch downgraded WBD in 2025, citing linear declines and leverage [17.15]; WBD paid **$2,295M of cash interest** in 2025 [17.16].

**Paramount Skydance (the acquirer).** Formed from the 2025 Skydance/Paramount Global merger, Paramount guided to **c. $30 billion of 2025 revenue** (and c. $30B in 2026, +4%) [18.1]. It exhibits the **same linear-decline / streaming-growth split** as its target: in Q3 2025 TV Media revenue fell 12% while Direct-to-Consumer grew 17%, led by a 24% rise in Paramount+ revenue [18.2]. **Paramount+ reached 79.1 million subscribers** (+13% YoY) [18.3], and management raised its run-rate cost-efficiency target from $2B to at least $3B [18.4]. Its stated strategy is explicitly to build "a global, scaled streaming platform" [18.5] — the thesis that motivates buying WBD.

## Industry & business model

The combination sits at the center of the **streaming endgame**. The global video-streaming market was ~**$160 billion in 2025**, forecast to c. $195.85B in 2026 (an 18.5% CAGR to 2035) [19.1], with the U.S. portion c. $47.6 billion [19.2]. The business model is a subscription-scale game: content is a largely fixed cost, so profitability turns on **spreading that content spend over the largest possible subscriber base**. On that axis the market is top-heavy.

| Streamer (latest disclosed) | Subscribers | Latest-quarter streaming profit | |
|---|---|---|---|
| Netflix | 325M | +$2.42B (rev +17.6%) | [20.1][20.2] |
| Disney+ / Hulu (+ ESPN+) | 195.7M (+24.1M) | +$450M | [20.3][20.4] |
| WBD (HBO Max) | c. 132M | +$393M (FY +$1.37B) | [20.5][20.6] |
| Paramount+ | 79M | −$158M (loss narrowing) | [20.7][20.8] |
| Peacock (Comcast) | 44M | unprofitable, widening losses | [20.9] |

Paramount+ and HBO Max are each **sub-scale relative to Netflix and Disney**. Combined they would reach **c. 200M+ subscribers** (Ellison's framing) [20.10] — roughly 0.65x Netflix's 325M base, so the merger **narrows but does not close** the gap to the leader. Critically, the two platforms sit at **opposite profitability points** — HBO Max profitable (c. $1.37B FY), Paramount+ still loss-making — which tempers a clean "profitable density" read of the combination.

**Precedent media M&A (the comparable set).** Sector mega-deals over the last decade have generally closed, with horizontal overlap remedied by divestiture rather than outright blocks:

| Deal | Year | Value | Outcome | |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| AT&T / Time Warner | 2018–19 | $85.4B | DOJ sued to block, **lost on appeal**, no conditions | [21.1][21.2] |
| Disney / 21st Century Fox | 2019 | c. $71B | cleared with **22 regional sports network divestiture** | [22][23] |
| Discovery / WarnerMedia | 2022 | c. $43B | Reverse Morris Trust — **created WBD itself** | [24.1][24.2] |
| Comcast / Sky | 2018 | c. $39B | won a rare three-round auction | [25] |
| Netflix / Warner Bros. | Dec 2025 | c. $82.7B | **superseded/abandoned** — the bid Paramount beat | [26] |

This precedent set establishes a base rate in which the binding antitrust constraint has been horizontal overlap (remedied by divestiture, as with Disney's ESPN+RSNs), not vertical-integration blocks.

## Deal rationale, the premium, and the Netflix flip

**The price is full.** $31.00 cash is a **147% premium** to the $12.54 unaffected price [8], i.e. roughly 2.5x the pre-auction level. The fairness opinion was a **joint** opinion of Allen & Company and J.P. Morgan; notably, WBD did **not** request and Evercore did **not** render a fairness opinion [5.11]. The advisors' sum-of-the-parts DCF produced an implied equity range of **$21.16–$31.91 per share** — so the $31.00 price sits **at the very top of intrinsic value**, leaving little standalone upside on the table, consistent with a contested-auction outcome rather than a discount entry [5.4].

**Why the board flipped from Netflix to Paramount.** The competing Netflix deal was **$27.75/share cash-and-stock** ($23.25 cash + $4.50 stock), c. $82.7B enterprise value, and acquired **only** the Streaming & Studios business after a Discovery Global spin-off [6.1]. Paramount's $31.00 all-cash whole-company bid delivered both **higher headline value (+11.7%)** and **cash certainty**, removing the stock-collar valuation risk the board flagged and covering 100% of the company. The WBD board's own stated reasons lead with exactly these points: an all-cash amount of at least $31.00 providing "certainty of value and liquidity" [5.12], with all-cash consideration removing valuation risk [5.12] — while acknowledging the trade-off that holders forgo future upside [5.10].

**The synergy case — asserted, then sized.** Paramount's February 27 announcement disclosed **no** dollar synergy figure, expressing both its 7.5x EBITDA valuation and its 4.3x leverage only "on a fully synergized basis" [1.2], with a purely qualitative basis (technology/infrastructure streamlining) [1.4] — meaning the headline multiple could not be independently checked at announcement. Management subsequently **quantified** the figure: Paramount expects **"over $6 billion" of synergies, phased c. 30% in year one and 70% by year two** [11], and CEO David Ellison reaffirmed the **$6 billion** number in March 2026, explicitly rebutting a larger figure Netflix's co-CEO had implied [27.1][27.2]. For contrast, the losing Netflix bid had disclosed **$2–3 billion** of annual cost savings and GAAP-EPS accretion by year two up front [6.2].

## Financing & balance sheet — the central tail risk

To fund an all-cash $110B deal, banks led by **Bank of America and Citigroup** committed roughly **$54–57.5 billion of new debt**, taking post-deal leverage to ~**6–6.5x EBITDA** [9]. The combined company is expected to carry **$79–87 billion of total debt** at close [9]. The structure evolved through early 2026: a **$49.0 billion senior secured bridge** giving way to permanent financing of c. $39.5B first-lien and $12.4B second-lien secured debt [11], with the bridge syndicated across 18 banks [28], $10.0B of permanent secured facilities put in place, and the unsecured revolver upsized to $5.0 billion [29][28]. A **$43.3 billion personal guarantee from Larry Ellison** backstops the equity financing [30].

**This will close as a junk-rated borrower.** S&P, which had already cut Paramount to junk, will lower the issuer rating to **'BB' at close** and does **not** expect leverage to fall below its 4.25x downgrade threshold until **2029** [10]. S&P treated Ellison's verbal leverage pledge as a tacit promise to inject capital if needed, after skeptics called the post-merger leverage "frightening" [31]. Management's deleveraging plan targets net debt/EBITDA below **3.75x by FY2028 and 3.0x by FY2029** [11] — but that path **rests on realizing the $6B of synergies against a structurally declining base**. A near-term pressure point: WBD carries ~**$16.5 billion of debt maturing in 2027** (within c. $32.7B of scheduled repayments), which the combined company must term out at junk spreads — partly pre-empted by refinancing WBD's $15B bridge [17.17][31]. Liquidity is supported by the $5.0B revolver plus WBD's c. $4.6B cash [17.18].

## Merger agreement & deal protections

The risk-allocation terms are markedly **seller-favorable**, signalling high buyer determination to close (from the DEFM14A "The Merger Agreement" summary and the Paramount 8-K):

| Provision | Term | |
|---|---|---|
| Company termination fee (WBD → PSKY) | $3 billion | [5.7] |
| Reverse regulatory fee (PSKY → WBD) | **$7 billion** | [5.8] |
| Netflix fee paid up front by PSKY | $2.8 billion (recoverable only on a WBD-side termination) | [5.3][5.8] |
| Ticking consideration | $0.25/share per quarter after Sept 30, 2026 | [5.6] |
| Outside date | March 4, 2027 + one automatic 3-month extension | [5.1][5.13] |
| Closing conditions | WBD vote, HSR/regulatory clearances, no restraining order | [5.14] |
| Regulatory-efforts standard | **reasonable best efforts, capped — NOT hell-or-high-water** | [5.15][5.16] |
| Financing condition | none — PSKY reps it will have the funds | [5.9] |
| Specific performance | available to WBD, no bond required | [5.17] |
| Deal protection | no-shop with a fiduciary out for a Superior Proposal | [5.18][5.19] |
| MAC/MAE | no Company MAE since Sept 30, 2025 represented | [5.20] |
| Sponsor backstop | Ellison Parties' guarantee delivered at signing | [5.21] |

The package shifts regulatory and timing risk decisively onto the buyer: the $7B reverse fee is ~**2.3x** the $3B WBD would owe, Paramount fronted the $2.8B Netflix fee, waived any financing condition, and granted specific performance. **But there is an important limit:** the regulatory-efforts covenant is a "reasonable best efforts" standard **expressly capped** so Paramount need not accept divestitures or remedies amounting to a material adverse effect — so its obligation to litigate or divest to clear antitrust review is bounded, a meaningful qualifier on close-certainty even with $7B at stake.

## Antitrust & regulatory

**Federal antitrust risk is resolved.** On **June 12, 2026 the DOJ Antitrust Division cleared the deal**, stating it "is not likely to result in harm to competition or American consumers" [3]; its eight-month review of 2M+ documents concluded the deal could even **strengthen** competition across streaming, linear TV and theatrical, with the combined company still facing larger rivals (Netflix, Amazon, Disney) [32.1][32.2]. Crucially the clearance was **unconditional — no divestitures, no behavioral remedies** [4]. The HSR path ran December 8, 2025 (filing) → Second Request → substantial-compliance certification Feb 9 → waiting period expired **February 19, 2026** [5.22][5.23]. Internationally, **Australia's ACCC cleared** the deal [33.1][33.2] and approvals came from a dozen-plus jurisdictions, though the **U.K. CMA opened a probe** that remained pending [33.3][33.4].

**Two regulatory risks remain live.** First, a **threatened multi-state attorneys-general lawsuit**: Bloomberg reported lawyers in c. 10 states drafting a complaint, with California's AG confirming the deal "remains under investigation," on a theory centered on the merged entity's leverage over creators and workers; state AGs retain **independent authority** to challenge even after DOJ clearance, and Senator Warren urged them to do so [13.1][14][32.3]. Second, an **FCC foreign-ownership review**: Paramount disclosed total foreign ownership of **49.5%, including 38.5% from Saudi, Qatari and Abu Dhabi funds**, requiring an FCC waiver above the 25% threshold [15.1]; Senate Democrats warned of an "unprecedented degree of foreign control of U.S. broadcasting" [15.2] and the FCC's lone Democrat called for "rigorous review" [15.3].

**Base rate.** The precedent set supports closing: AT&T–Time Warner cleared over a DOJ court loss with no conditions, and Disney–Fox cleared via a 22-RSN divestiture — i.e. media mega-deals close, with state-AG litigation and the pending FCC/CMA reviews, not federal antitrust, now the residual gating risks.

## Management, governance & control

**Control concentrates in one person.** David Ellison holds **100% of the Ellison family's voting interests**, is Chairman and CEO, and is sole manager of the family entities that control National Amusements and the combined Paramount [34.1]; the family's vehicle owns 77.5% of NAI (RedBird 22.5%) and appoints up to five of seven NAI directors [34.2][34.3]. The leadership bench is set: **Jeff Shell** (ex-RedBird, ex-NBCUniversal) as president [35], **Cindy Holland** (ex-Netflix) over streaming [36], **George Cheeks** over TV Media [36] — and, notably, WBD's **David Zaslav is slated to stay** as President & CEO of the combined Streaming & Studios business [5.24]. The combination therefore carries concentrated **key-person/control risk** around Ellison, who personally fronts the editorial-independence pledges.

**The Zaslav payout is a governance flashpoint.** WBD's proxy quantifies Zaslav's golden-parachute compensation at **$886,836,175** — including $517M of equity and a **$335M tax gross-up** [5.25]. The bulk vests on a **single trigger** (closing alone, regardless of employment) [5.26]; the gross-up was added days before the proxy and drew Glass Lewis criticism [2]. Shareholders **voted down** the (advisory, non-binding) pay proposal even as they approved the merger — so the package is payable anyway [5.27][2]. Zaslav also sold $114M of WBD stock shortly after Paramount won [2], and the proxy discloses the Ellisons had signaled a "several hundred million dollar" package to him during the process [5.28].

**Foreign ownership and editorial independence** are the distinct governance pressure point: the 49.5% foreign / 38.5% Gulf-fund profile triggers the FCC waiver and Senate objection, against which Paramount's only mitigant is the Ellison family's retained voting control and the investors' asserted passivity [15.4]. Amid concern over common ownership of CNN and CBS News, Ellison **pledged** CNN's editorial independence "needs to be maintained" [37] — an assurance critics question, citing his $150M purchase of The Free Press and installation of Bari Weiss atop CBS News [37].

## Operational profile & integration

The synergy lever runs through **streaming-platform and back-office consolidation**, not content or headcount cuts. Paramount plans to **combine Paramount+ and HBO Max into one service** (preserving the HBO brand) if the deal closes [38], targeting **up to $6 billion** of cost synergies over three years that chief strategy officer Andy Gordon says will **not** include layoffs or content reductions [12]. The asserted playbook is the tech-stack convergence Paramount is already running internally (merging Paramount+, BET+ and Pluto onto one stack) as a "rehearsal" for WBD [39][40] — so the synergy thesis is **execution-dependent on an integration the company has only begun**.

Three facts temper the upside. First, the c. 200M combined-subscriber figure **overstates net reach**: Antenna estimates **7.6M U.S. subscribers already hold both** services, and Parrot Analytics finds an HBO Max viewer is 86.7% as likely to watch a Paramount+ title as a core P+ viewer — so deduplication onto one app carries **subscriber-attrition risk** rather than pure additive growth [41][42]. Second, the "no layoffs" framing sits in tension with management's own acknowledgment that corporate overhead "will absolutely have to" be rationalized — both companies having already absorbed prior layoff waves, raising the question of how much non-content overhead remains to cut. Third, as of the March 2026 announcement Paramount had **not disclosed pricing or naming** for the combined app [38].

## Macro & political overhang

**The secular decline of linear is the macro fact that matters.** WBD's FY2025 distribution revenue fell 8% on a 9% drop in domestic linear subscribers, and advertising revenue fell 11% on **25% linear audience declines** — so the demand base for the Global Linear Networks segment Paramount is levering up to buy is eroding at a **double-digit annual pace** [17.19][17.20]. Independent data corroborates: U.S. pay-TV penetration has fallen to c. 50% (from c. 80% in 2018), with households down 6% YoY [43]. WBD itself warns there is **no assurance** streaming growth will replace structural linear decline [17.21]. The combined entity is also exposed to **advertising cyclicality** [17.22][17.23] and, given c. $50B+ of new debt, to **rising interest rates** via its variable-rate bridge [17.24].

**Politics is a genuine, two-sided transmission channel.** The deal is bankrolled by Trump ally Larry Ellison, whose son runs Paramount, and CNN reports the close ties to the administration as a controversy [13.2]. That alignment **plausibly eased** the DOJ's no-conditions clearance, but it simultaneously creates residual risk — the threatened state-AG challenge [13.2], the unresolved FCC foreign-ownership review [15.5][15.6], and a **post-close editorial-independence controversy** over commonly owned CNN and CBS News, sharpened by the FCC "capitulation over courage" dissent and Paramount's prior overhaul of CBS News / "60 Minutes" [44][13.2].

## The contest & the bear case

There is **no classic short-seller report** on this situation; the analogue shareholder-pressure dynamic was a **two-sided contested takeover** [7]. After WBD agreed to Netflix, Paramount went hostile, urged shareholders to vote against the Netflix deal and to tender [45.1][45.2]; the WBD board pushed back, **reaffirming Netflix and recommending shareholders reject Paramount's "inferior" bid** [46.1][46.2], and claimed 93%+ of shareholders had rejected it [47] — until the board reversed when Paramount escalated to $31.00 [48].

**The most developed bear thesis is a leverage/financeability case** — and it came from the WBD board itself while opposing the bid. WBD argued Paramount (c. $14B market cap) was funding **c. $94.65 billion** of debt and equity — nearly 7x its own market cap [46.3] — in what it called "the largest LBO in history," with c. $87B of pro-forma gross debt and ~**7x 2026E EBITDA before synergies** [46.3]. Because the proponent was an interested party, the file corroborates the thesis directionally from two independent directions: **Netflix walked away** at $31, stating the deal was "no longer financially attractive" [49.1], and **Paramount itself cast doubt on the value of WBD's cable/linear assets** even as it bid for the whole company [49.2].

## Litigation & legal

The litigation profile is **process- and deal-driven, not operational**. The contested process generated several suits: **Paramount itself sued WBD's board** in the Delaware Court of Chancery during the auction (expedition denied) [17.25]; a stockholder suit alleged 14D-9 disclosure failures [17.25]; and a merger-objection suit (*Nicosia*) plus **15 stockholder demand letters** target the proxy [50.1][50.2]. WBD also carries standing pre-merger litigation — a securities class action (*Collura*, SDNY, Nov 2024) and a stayed derivative action [17.26][17.27] — and flags that further deal-related suits may be filed [17.28]. A key contract underpinning certainty is the **Ellison/trust guarantee** of $45.72B of merger consideration [17.29].

These disclosure/process claims are typically settled via supplemental disclosure rather than capable of blocking a deal; the genuine close risk is the **threatened multi-state attorneys-general antitrust action**, which — coming after a no-conditions DOJ clearance — is the one piece of litigation that could seek to **enjoin closing** [13.3][13.4].

## Preliminary read & coverage

**What the facts establish (for the IC memo to weigh).** This is a strategically coherent — but **expensively financed** — bid to build a No. 2 streaming platform by consolidating two sub-scale streamers and two major studios. The price is full: $31.00 is a 147% premium and sits at the top of the advisors' DCF range, so the economics lean on realizing **"over $6B"** of cost synergies [11] while carrying c. $79–87B of debt at junk ratings against a **declining linear core** [9][10]. **Deal certainty splits cleanly:** the vote is done [2] and federal antitrust is cleared unconditionally, but residual close risk concentrates in the **state-AG challenge** and the **FCC foreign-ownership review** [13.1][15.1] — against which Paramount's $7B reverse fee and high determination are offset by a **bounded, non-hell-or-high-water** efforts covenant. The "will it close" and "is the price/leverage sound" questions should be argued separately in the memo.

**Coverage.** 11 of 12 checklist areas are covered with verified claims; the one not searched — ESG/environmental — was deliberately scoped out (immaterial environmental liability for a media company; the live social/governance risks are captured under governance and macro). The coverage map reports **0 required/recommended gaps**, and no covered area was flagged thin.

**Honest limitations from the research pass:**
- **Synergy basis:** the $6B figure is grounded as management's assertion (with a 30%/70% phasing), but the filings give **no cost-vs-revenue split and no dollar breakdown** by tech vs. overhead vs. content — so the precise mechanism remains a reasoned judgment for the memo, not a cited number.
- **Pro-forma leverage** is reported at c. 6–6.5x by independent sources [9] vs. c. 4.3x management framing — the latter expressed "on a fully synergized basis"; the management 4.3x claim and its reconciliation **quarantined** on sourcing (the figure survives via S&P's verified "not below 4.25x until 2029" [10]).
- **FCC status:** an earlier 2025 FCC broadcast-license approval (2-1, with a dissent) related to the **Skydance/CBS** transaction, **not** this WBD deal; for the WBD deal the FCC foreign-ownership review was **still pending** as of June 2026 — the brief treats it as unresolved [15.1][15.3].
- **Quarantines (5 of 220 proposals):** Paramount's own segment breakdown and one management-leverage datapoint quarantined on sourcing; both are backstopped by verified claims elsewhere (acquirer revenue [18.1]; leverage [9][10]), so no load-bearing fact was lost.
