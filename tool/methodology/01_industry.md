# Card 01 — Industry structure & economics

**Bucket:** Industry
**The why it answers:** How does this industry make money, where do profit pools sit, and
what governs profitability — concentration, pricing power, supply chain, trends?
**Grounded in:** Porter's Five Forces; Porter's Value Chain; industry life cycle.

## First-principles trace (do this FIRST)
- How does the industry make money at the unit level (carry card 00's profit-engine read up
  to the whole industry)?
- Who are the players — how many national-scale operators vs. a regional long tail? How
  concentrated is the market (rough share of the top few)?
- Where does **pricing power** lie — with the operators, their customers, or their suppliers?
  Why?
- How does the **supply chain** work, and what does it cost to serve an incremental customer?
- What **big trends** (secular demand, technology, labor, input costs) are reshaping it?

## Framework prompts (test the trace — adapt or discard)
- **Five forces:** rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, buyer power, supplier power — each as
  a *sourced fact*, not an adjective. Which force most caps industry profitability?
- **Value chain:** which activities create the value / hold the cost? Where could scale,
  density, or integration change the economics?
- **Life cycle:** is this growth / shakeout / mature / declining? (Consolidation clusters in
  shakeout and maturity — a mature, slow-growth industry is *expected* to consolidate, which
  is itself evidence on the deal's logic.)

## Facts to gather (→ research)
- Market size / structure; number and rough share of major players; concentration.
- Cost structure of a typical operator; what scales with customers/volume.
- Pricing dynamics; supplier and customer concentration.
- Secular trends with a number where possible (growth rate, penetration, input-cost moves).

## Inferences to form (→ expert)
- The one force or structural fact that most governs profitability here.
- Where the industry sits in its life cycle and what that implies for M&A.
- Whether scale/density/integration plausibly changes the economics (sets up cards 03/05).

## Weak-answer tells
- "Highly competitive / fragmented" with no concentration number.
- Cost structure described but never tied to what a merger could change.
- Treating "bigger = better" with no density/utilization/pricing mechanism.
